# Introduction and Historical Context

# **A Master's Legacy** {#a-master's-legacy}

In the opening pages of his manuscript, an aging swordsman begins his story:

"I, Fiore de'i Liberi of Cividale in the diocese of Aquileia, have studied the art of arms for more than forty years. And I have also trained with many masters, from various lands..."\[1\]

This is how we meet Fiore dei Liberi. Not through records of his birth or noble genealogy, but through his own voice, speaking across six centuries to introduce himself and his life's work.

He was, by his own account, undefeated. He fought in judicial duels, trial by combat where the law settled disputes with steel. He trained at the courts of Italian nobility. He studied under multiple masters and developed rivals who challenged his reputation. And late in his career, sometime around 1409, he decided to write it all down.

---

# **Who: The Man Behind the Manuscript** {#who:-the-man-behind-the-manuscript}

### **Early Life and Training** {#early-life-and-training}

Fiore dei Liberi was born sometime in the 1350s or early 1360s in northeastern Italy, likely in or near Premariacco in the region of Friuli.\[2\] The exact date is lost to history, but we can reconstruct his timeline from his own words.

He tells us he studied the art of arms for over forty years before writing his treatise.\[3\] If the Getty manuscript dates to around 1410, this places the beginning of his training in the 1360s or early 1370s, when he would have been a young man, perhaps twelve to fifteen years old.

Unlike many medieval martial artists, Fiore wasn't content with a single teacher. He sought out multiple masters across different regions:

"And I have also trained with many masters, from various lands, namely with Master Johannes called Suveno of Germany, and with Nicholai de Toblem of Germany, and with Master Johanne ditto Rosso of Padua..."\[4\]

**\[INSERT IMAGE PLACEHOLDER: Map of Northern Italy showing key locations: Cividale, Padua, Ferrara, Udine\]**

This was uncommon. Most students trained under one master in one tradition. But Fiore was building something comprehensive. He was learning different approaches, techniques,  and ways of understanding combat.

### **The Competitive World of Masters at Arms** {#the-competitive-world-of-masters-at-arms}

Medieval martial arts was a fiercely competitive profession. Masters didn't just teach. They fought to prove their systems worked. Fiore describes this world vividly:

"And so, because many masters of this art, through envy, disparaged me, I had to fight with one on foot with sharp spear, and with another with sharp sword..."\[5\]

He's referring to challenges, formal combats where masters fought each other to establish superiority. These weren't friendly sparring matches. They were serious fights with sharp weapons—sometimes to the death, sometimes under controlled conditions—but always with reputation on the line.

Fiore claims he won every challenge:

"...and by the grace of God, I was never injured, nor did I lose honor in any fight..."\[6\]

Was he exaggerating? Possibly. But he's writing for an audience who could verify his claims: nobles who knew him, students who trained with him, and rivals who fought him.

### **The Scope of Fiore's Experience** {#the-scope-of-fiore's-experience}

It's important to understand what kind of fighting Fiore specialized in. His claims of being undefeated refer specifically to judicial duels and formal challenges with other masters. These were one-on-one combats with sharp weapons where skill and technique were paramount.

Fiore doesn't mention battlefield experience, and this makes sense given his role. As a master at arms serving noble courts, he was a specialist in personal combat. He trained knights and nobles in the techniques they'd use in duels, tournaments, and self-defense situations.\[7\]

Think about the difference between a modern Olympic fencing coach and a military combatives instructor. Both teach fighting, but they're preparing students for completely different contexts. Fiore was the former, not the latter. He wasn't teaching how to hold a shield wall or fight in formation. He was teaching what a knight needed to know for a judicial duel, for defending himself on a dangerous road, for a challenge of honor. Situations where individual technique and skill mattered more than mass tactics.

This is exactly what the longsword section preserves: a complete system for personal combat. When you learn from Fiore, you're learning the same skills a 14th-century knight would have needed when he faced another armed man alone.

### **Service to Nobility** {#service-to-nobility}

Fiore's career took him to several important courts in Northern Italy. He specifically mentions training at the court of Niccolò III d'Este, Marquis of Ferrara, one of the most powerful nobles of the period.\[8\]

This tells us something important: Fiore wasn't teaching common soldiers or city militia. He was training knights and nobles, men who could afford the best instruction, men who might actually face judicial combat or need to defend their honor with steel.

His students weren't learning for sport or exercise. They were learning because they might have to fight for real. In judicial duels. In tournaments. In self-defense on dangerous roads. The stakes were high, and the instruction needed to be effective.

### **Northern Italy: The Geographic Setting** {#northern-italy:-the-geographic-setting}

Fiore's career centered on northeastern Italy, the regions of Friuli, Veneto, and Emilia-Romagna. This put him in a unique position.

North of him lay the German martial traditions of Liechtenauer and the German masters. South lay the traditions of central Italy. To the east were routes to Eastern Europe. Fiore synthesized influences from multiple sources, creating something that drew from many wells of knowledge.\[12\]

The cities where he worked, Ferrara, Mantua, Udine, maintained sophisticated courts. Noble households employed masters at arms as permanent staff. Training in martial arts was part of noble education, taught alongside music, languages, and courtly manners.

# **Why: The Motivations for Writing** {#why:-the-motivations-for-writing}

### **Preserving Knowledge** {#preserving-knowledge}

By around 1409, Fiore was in his fifties or sixties, old for the medieval period, especially for a fighting man. He'd spent four decades mastering his art and he realized that knowledge could die with him.

His manuscript, The Flower of Battle wasn't written on a whim. It was a deliberate act of preservation:

"And because this art and this knowledge can be lost through death, which can befall everyone, I, Fiore...have written this book..."\[13\]

He wanted his students to have a reference. He wanted future generations to learn what he'd learned. He wanted his legacy to outlive his body. The desire to leave something behind is a universal experience,  but for Fiore it was particularly urgent. His knowledge was practical, physical, and embodied. Without a written record, it would vanish with the last person who remembered his teaching.

### **Establishing Authority** {#establishing-authority}

In addition to preserving knowledge, competition was another  motive. Fiore faced rivals who disparaged his methods:

"...and because many who don't know me say that I'm not a good master..."\[14\]

He's writing, in part, to prove himself. The manuscript is both a teaching manual and a resume. Look at this system. Look at these techniques. Look at what I know. Look at what I can teach.

This competitive edge serves us well. It meant Fiore had to be thorough, had to show his complete system, had to prove his methods worked. We benefit from his need to establish his credentials.

### **Creating a Complete System** {#creating-a-complete-system}

Fiore wasn't just documenting random techniques, he was presenting a complete fighting system.

It starts with four principles, or  virtues, represented by animals. It builds through wrestling, the foundation of all combat,  then progresses through weapons \- dagger, sword, poleaxe, and spear. It even includes mounted combat. Everything is organized, systematic, and teachable.

This is why The Flower of Battle is so valuable to us today. It's not a grab-bag of tricks or  a collection of isolated techniques. It's a complete martial art from a master who spent a lifetime perfecting it, presented in a way that's designed to be taught and learned.

# **What: The Fior di Battaglia Manuscripts** {#what:-the-fior-di-battaglia-manuscripts}

### **Four Versions Survive** {#four-versions-survive}

Fiore's work survives in four manuscripts, each slightly different.\[15\]

**The Getty Manuscript (MS Ludwig XV 13\)**, created around 1410, is the version we focus on. With approximately 280 illustrations, it is the most preserved and complete text.. Now housed in the Getty Museum in Los Angeles, it's been fully digitized and is publicly accessible.\[16\] 

**The Morgan Manuscript (MS M.383)** is a more condensed version with fewer illustrations and shorter text. However, it contains some unique techniques not found in the Getty version. It resides in the Morgan Library in New York.

**The Pisani-Dossi Manuscript** is similar to the Getty but remains in a private collection with limited public access.

**The Paris Manuscript (MS Latin 11269\)** is also similar to Getty, with some variations in illustrations. It's housed in the Bibliothèque Nationale de France.

### **Why We Use the Getty** {#why-we-use-the-getty}

For this manual, we draw primarily from the Getty manuscript for several reasons. It's the most complete. The illustrations are the clearest. The text is most extensive. It's publicly available for anyone to study. And it represents Fiore's most fully developed presentation of his art.

**\[INSERT IMAGE PLACEHOLDER: Example page from Getty manuscript showing the segno with four animals and seven swords\]**

### **What's Inside** {#what's-inside}

The Flower of Battle covers an enormous range of topics.

It begins with foundational concepts: the segno, the diagram of the four virtues, and principles that apply to all combat regardless of weapon.

Then comes unarmed combat, wrestling- throws, locks, and breaks. This is the foundation for understanding all other weapons. If you understand how bodies move and balance, you understand how to control them whether you're armed or not.

The dagger section covers fights where both combatants are armed, and defenses against dagger attacks.

The longsword section, our focus in this manual, includes the twelve guards, the seven blows, plays (techniques) from each guard, and remedies (counters) to each play.

There are sections on sword in one hand, with and without buckler, both mounted and on foot. Poleaxe techniques for fighting in armor. Spear and lance work, on foot and mounted. And mounted combat with lance, sword, and wrestling on horseback.

This breadth is remarkable. Fiore wasn't a specialist. He was teaching complete martial competence, everything a knight might need across every context he might fight in.

# **Why It Matters for Us** {#why-it-matters-for-us}

### **A Window Into Medieval Combat** {#a-window-into-medieval-combat}

The Flower of Battle gives us something rare: not the romanticized duels of later fiction or the cinematic battles of film, but a detailed, systematic record of the practical, effective techniques of a master who trained fighting men. When we study this manuscript, we're looking at what worked when the stakes were life and death.

### **A Complete Teaching System** {#a-complete-teaching-system}

Fiore wasn't just showing techniques. He was showing how to teach them. That progression follows a deliberate structure.

First, understand the principles, the four virtues. Then learn the positions, the guards. Then learn the actions, the blows. Then learn the applications, the plays. Finally, learn the counters, the remedies.

This pedagogical structure makes his system uniquely valuable for modern instructors. He thought about how knowledge transfers from teacher to student and he built that understanding into his manuscript.

### **Timeless Principles** {#timeless-principles}

While the specific context of fighting judicial duels has changed, we don't fight judicial duels anymore, the principles Fiore teaches remain valid.

The four virtues apply to any martial art. The tactical relationships between offense and defense, between distance and timing, and between commitment and caution, are timeless. The biomechanics of striking and grappling haven't changed in six hundred years. 

A fencer today who cultivates prudence, audacity, celerity, and fortitude will be more effective than one who doesn't, regardless of the weapon or ruleset.

### **Fiore's Voice** {#fiore's-voice}

Remarkably, Fiore's own words. In a pedagogical innovation that's both unusual and brilliant, he has the guards themselves speak:

"I am Posta di Donna...I can make all seven blows..."\[17\]

This isn't dry technical writing. It's vivid, memorable, and designed to stick in the reader’s minds. When you teach Posta di Donna and you quote what the guard says about itself, you're using the same mnemonic device Fiore used six centuries ago.

# **Fiore Di Libre’s Animals**  {#fiore-di-libre’s-animals}

Before getting into the main focus of this piece, I want to quickly go over four animals and the types of qualities people usually associate with them. This isn't meant to analyze or explain anything yet. J This is to put some basic ideas in the reader's mind so they're already there when these animals show up later with more context.

The four animals are the lynx, tiger, elephant, and lion.

**Lynx.** The lynx is a medium-sized wild cat, roughly about the size of a big dog. It's not an animal most people think about often (I had to look it up myself), but it's usually associated with sharp senses and quiet awareness. Rather than relying on size or strength, the lynx tends to suggest observation, patience, and precision.

**Tiger.** Tigers almost immediately bring to mind speed, stealth, and accuracy. They're powerful, but what really stands out is how controlled they are—waiting, watching, and then striking fast when the moment is right.

**Elephant.** Elephants suggest strength in a very different way. They're massive, slow-moving, and incredibly solid. When people think of elephants, it's usually in terms of stability, endurance, and sheer presence rather than quick or aggressive action.

**Lion.** The lion is probably the most familiar of the four. It's commonly seen as being at the top of the food chain and is often linked with dominance, authority, and a commanding presence.

# **What Fiore Actually Meant** {#what-fiore-actually-meant}

## **The Segno and Medieval Bestiaries** {#the-segno-and-medieval-bestiaries}

The famous image appears at the beginning of Fiore's longsword section: a fencing master surrounded by seven swords and four animals. This diagram, called the *segno* (sign), is one of the most recognizable images from medieval martial arts manuscripts. The animals come from medieval bestiaries. Which are encyclopedic collections of real and mythical creatures that were used to teach moral and philosophical lessons.

Fiore explicitly states that these four animals represent the four essential virtues of a fighter: **Prudence, Audacity, Fortitude, and Celerity**. Each animal was deliberately chosen based on what medieval people believed about these creatures' nature and behavior.

## **The Lynx — Prudentia (Prudence)** {#the-lynx-—-prudentia-(prudence)}

The lynx sits above the master's head, holding a compass (or calipers). From the Getty manuscript:

"No creature sees better than me, the Lynx. And I always set things in order with compass and measure."

In medieval thought, the lynx was believed to have extraordinary vision — so keen it could see through walls. This wasn't about literal eyesight; it represented **judgment, perception, and strategic thinking**.

The compass reinforces this: it's a tool for measurement and precision. The lynx embodies the fighter's ability to read distance, timing, and the opponent's intentions. This is about **accuracy, timing, and distance judgment** — knowing exactly when and where to act.

Fiore places the lynx *above* the master's head because prudence governs all other virtues. Without good judgment, speed becomes recklessness, strength becomes clumsiness, and courage becomes foolishness.

## **The Lion — Audatia (Audacity/Courage)** {#the-lion-—-audatia-(audacity/courage)}

The lion sits to the master's right (viewer's left), holding a heart beneath its paw. The manuscript describes audacity as:

*"Audacity, such virtue is what this art is all about."*

The lion represents **courage and boldness** — the willingness to commit to action and enter into the fight. The heart symbol makes this explicit: this is about having the courage to engage, to take risks, and to press forward when the moment demands it.

However, Fiore is careful about placement. The lion is positioned to the *side* rather than at the top, suggesting that while audacity is essential ("what this art is all about"), it must be tempered by the other virtues. Courage without prudence is suicide; courage without speed or stability cannot execute properly.

## **The Tiger — Celeritas (Speed/Quickness)** {#the-tiger-—-celeritas-(speed/quickness)}

The tiger sits to the master's left (viewer's right), holding an arrow. Medieval bestiaries described tigers as incredibly swift creatures, and the text reinforces this:

*"The tiger with an arrow represents speed and decision, as a tiger never retreats."*

The arrow is the symbol of speed — nothing moves faster. But this isn't just about physical quickness. It's also about **decisiveness and commitment**. The phrase "a tiger never retreats" captures the idea of forward momentum and the mental speed to assess and act without hesitation.

This combines both **physical speed** (the swiftness of execution) and **intellectual speed** (rapidly assessing the situation and adapting). When an opportunity presents itself, you must be able to move *now* — both in body and mind.

## **The Elephant — Fortitudo (Strength/Fortitude)** {#the-elephant-—-fortitudo-(strength/fortitude)}

The elephant sits beneath the master's feet, carrying a castle or siege tower on its back. From the Getty manuscript:

"I am the elephant and I have a castle for a burden. And never do I kneel down nor do I lose my true place."

Medieval bestiaries claimed that elephants had no knees. If they fell down, they couldn't get back up and would die. This was false zoology, but powerful symbolism.

The elephant doesn't primarily represent muscular power. Instead, it represents **strong and balanced posture, stability, and an unshakeable foundation**. The castle reinforces this — it's a structure that cannot be moved, a defensive position that holds firm.

This is about maintaining your structure under pressure, keeping your balance, and not being displaced or overwhelmed. "Never do I kneel" means you don't collapse under load. "I lose not my footing" means you stay rooted and stable.

# **Why These Animals?** {#why-these-animals?}

## **Medieval Bestiaries as Teaching Tools** {#medieval-bestiaries-as-teaching-tools}

In the medieval world, bestiaries were far more than natural history books. They were moral and philosophical texts that used animals as symbols to teach abstract concepts. Every educated person of Fiore's time would have been familiar with these symbolic meanings.

By using bestiary animals, Fiore was tapping into a shared cultural vocabulary. His students — nobles at the court of Niccolò III d'Este — would have immediately understood the associations. These weren't random choices; they were carefully selected symbols that carried specific meanings his audience already knew.

## **The Balance of Virtues** {#the-balance-of-virtues}

Fiore's genius was in recognizing that these four qualities must work *in balance*. None can dominate without undermining the others:

* **Prudence without courage** leads to hesitation and missed opportunities  
* **Courage without prudence** leads to reckless charges and poor decisions  
* **Speed without strength** produces weak, ineffective actions  
* **Strength without speed** makes you slow and predictable

The segno shows all four animals surrounding the master equally. They must all be present, all developed, and all balanced against each other. This isn't just about swordsmanship — this is Fiore's philosophy of how to *live* as a martial artist.

# **Fiore's Seven Blows (Sette Colpi)**

---

## **Introduction: The Foundation of All Techniques** {#introduction:-the-foundation-of-all-techniques}

Fiore dei Liberi's longsword system is built on **seven fundamental blows** (*sette colpi*). Every technique, every play, every remedy in the Getty manuscript uses some combination of these seven strikes. Master these seven, and you have the vocabulary to execute the entire system.

As Posta di Donna declares: *"I can make all seven blows"* — this is not boasting, but a statement of completeness. Understanding the seven blows is understanding how to attack from any position, at any angle.

---

## **The Seven Blows: Overview** {#the-seven-blows:-overview}

### **The Pattern** {#the-pattern}

Fiore organizes strikes into **three angles, two sides each, plus one centerline thrust:**

**Descending Cuts (Fendente):**

1. Fendente (destra) — Right descending diagonal  
2. Fendente sinistro — Left descending diagonal

**Middle Cuts (Mezzano):**

1. Mezzano (destro) — Right horizontal cut  
2. Mezzano sinistro — Left horizontal cut

**Rising Cuts (Sottano):**

1. Sottano (destro) — Right rising diagonal  
2. Sottano sinistro — Left rising diagonal

**Thrust:**

1. Punta — Centerline thrust

### **Why This Organization?** {#why-this-organization?}

**Complete Coverage:** These seven blows cover every possible attack angle — high, middle, low, from both sides, plus the thrust.

**Bilateral Development:** By requiring proficiency with cuts from both right and left, Fiore ensures complete martial development.

**Guard Integration:** Each blow begins in one guard and ends in another, creating the flow that connects the entire system.

**Strategic Clarity:** Naming seven specific blows gives you a clear framework for understanding combat.

---

# **Lesson 1: Fendente (Destra)** {#lesson-1:-fendente-(destra)}

**The Descending Cut from the Right**

## **Physical Description** {#physical-description}

**Path of the Blade:**

* Starts high on the right side (typically from Posta di Donna Destra)  
* Travels diagonally downward  
* Cuts from right shoulder toward opponent's left hip/shoulder  
* Approximately 45-degree angle

**Body Mechanics:**

* Right foot typically steps forward during delivery  
* Hips rotate to add power  
* Weight shifts forward  
* Shoulders follow the blade

**Ending Position:**

* Typically finishes in Dente di Zenghiaro (low guard)  
* Can finish in Porta di Ferro Mezana (middle)  
* Blade ends low, point forward

## **Tactical Function** {#tactical-function}

**Primary Target:** Head, neck, shoulder, arm (descending from above)

**Power Generation:** The descending angle plus body weight creates tremendous impact

**Guard Breaking:** This is the "great blow" (*gran colpi*) Posta di Donna mentions, powerful enough to break through other guards

**Range:** Long reach due to the diagonal path and forward step

## **Connection to Guards** {#connection-to-guards}

**Primary Starting Guards:**

* Posta di Donna Destra (most common)  
* Posta Breve (quick version)  
* Posta Frontale/Corona (maximum power version)

**Ending Guards:**

* Dente di Zenghiaro (most common)  
* Porta di Ferro Mezana (middle height)

## **Connection to the Four Virtues** {#connection-to-the-four-virtues}

* **Lion (Audacity):** Full commitment to a powerful, decisive strike  
* **Tiger (Celerity):** Must be delivered with speed despite the power  
* **Elephant (Fortitude):** Solid structure throughout the cut  
* **Lynx (Prudence):** Accurate targeting and distance judgment

## **Drill 1: Solo Cutting** {#drill-1:-solo-cutting}

**Basic Form:**

1. Adopt Posta di Donna Destra (right shoulder, blade chambered)  
2. Step forward with right foot  
3. Release the cut, letting the blade fall diagonally  
4. Follow through completely  
5. Finish in Dente di Zenghiaro  
6. Repeat 20 times, focusing on smooth acceleration

**Key Points:**

* Let gravity help — don't muscle the cut  
* Hips and shoulders rotate together  
* The blade should accelerate through the strike  
* Complete follow-through to the low guard

## **Drill 2: Targeting** {#drill-2:-targeting}

**With Partner (Slow, No Contact):**

1. Partner A in Posta di Donna Destra  
2. Partner B stands in neutral guard  
3. A delivers slow fendente, targeting B's left shoulder  
4. B observes the blade path  
5. Repeat 10 times  
6. Switch roles

**Targets to Practice:**

* Left temple/head  
* Left shoulder/collarbone  
* Left arm (especially if raised)  
* Descending to left hip

## **Common Errors** {#common-errors}

**Error:** Stopping the cut at contact point (in solo practice)

**Fix:** Follow through to the low guard; the cut doesn't stop at the target

**Error:** Pulling the cut (not committing)

**Fix:** Let the blade travel its full arc with acceleration

**Error:** All arms, no body

**Fix:** Hips and shoulders must rotate; power comes from the whole body

---

# **Lesson 2: Fendente Sinistro** {#lesson-2:-fendente-sinistro}

**The Descending Cut from the Left**

## **Physical Description** {#physical-description-1}

**Path of the Blade:**

* Starts high on the left side (from Posta di Donna La Sinestra)  
* Travels diagonally downward  
* Cuts from left shoulder toward opponent's right hip/shoulder  
* Mirror image of Fendente (destra)

**Body Mechanics:**

* Left foot typically steps forward during delivery  
* Hips rotate in opposite direction from the right fendente  
* Weight shifts forward  
* Mirror mechanics of the right-side cut

## **Tactical Function** {#tactical-function-1}

**Primary Target:** Head, neck, right shoulder, right arm

**Strategic Use:** Attacking from the opposite side forces the opponent to defend a different line

**Combination:** Often used in sequence with fendente (destra) to attack both sides alternately

## **Drill 1: Bilateral Development** {#drill-1:-bilateral-development}

**Solo:**

1. Posta di Donna Destra → Fendente → Zenghiaro  
2. Flow to Posta di Donna La Sinestra  
3. Fendente Sinistro → Zenghiaro  
4. Flow back to Donna Destra  
5. Repeat 10 complete cycles (20 cuts total)

**Goal:** Equal proficiency on both sides

## **Drill 2: Alternating Sides** {#drill-2:-alternating-sides}

**With Partner (Light Contact):**

1. A delivers fendente (destra) toward B's left side  
2. A immediately flows to left side  
3. A delivers fendente sinistro toward B's right side  
4. B must defend against cuts from both angles  
5. Repeat 10 times, then switch roles

**Tactical Lesson:** Attacking from alternating sides creates confusion and prevents the opponent from settling into a defensive pattern

## **Common Errors** {#common-errors-1}

**Error:** Weaker or slower on non-dominant side

**Fix:** Practice left-side cuts more frequently until equal to right side

**Error:** Different mechanics on left vs. right

**Fix:** Film yourself or use a mirror; ensure identical form on both sides

---

# **Lesson 3: Mezzano (Destro)** {#lesson-3:-mezzano-(destro)}

**The Right Horizontal Cut**

## **Physical Description** {#physical-description-2}

**Path of the Blade:**

* Travels horizontally from right to left  
* Middle height (chest to neck level)  
* Roughly parallel to the ground  
* Can also target arms or legs at different heights

**Body Mechanics:**

* Rotating cut powered by hip turn  
* Less reliance on gravity than fendente  
* More rotational mechanics

**Ending Position:**

* Often finishes in Posta di Fenestra La Sinestra (left window)  
* Or continues rotating through to another guard

## **Tactical Function** {#tactical-function-2}

**Primary Targets:**

* Neck/throat (high mezzano)  
* Ribs/torso (middle mezzano)  
* Arms (if extended)  
* Legs/knees (low mezzano)

**Speed vs. Power:** Generally faster than fendente but with less gravitational assistance

**Rotational Flow:** Excellent for circular footwork and continuous movement

## **Connection to Guards** {#connection-to-guards-1}

**Primary Starting Guards:**

* Posta di Fenestra Destra (horizontal from extended position)  
* Posta di Donna Destra (can rotate into horizontal)  
* Porta di Ferro Mezana (middle position)

**Ending Guards:**

* Posta di Fenestra La Sinestra (opposite side window)  
* Can continue rotation to Donna La Sinestra

## **Drill 1: Hip Rotation** {#drill-1:-hip-rotation}

**Solo:**

1. Adopt Posta di Fenestra Destra  
2. Rotate hips to the left, blade following horizontally  
3. Finish in Fenestra La Sinestra (opposite window)  
4. Reverse: rotate hips right, blade travels back  
5. Repeat 10 times each direction

**Focus:** The cut is powered by hip rotation, not just arm swing

## **Drill 2: Target Variation** {#drill-2:-target-variation}

**With Partner (Slow, No Contact):**

1. Partner B holds various target positions  
2. A delivers mezzano at different heights:  
   * High (neck)  
   * Middle (ribs)  
   * Low (thigh/knee)  
3. Repeat 5 times at each height  
4. Switch roles

## **Common Errors** {#common-errors-2}

**Error:** Swinging with arms only

**Fix:** Initiate the cut with hip rotation; arms follow

**Error:** Blade drifting up or down during cut

**Fix:** Maintain consistent height throughout the horizontal path

---

# **Lesson 4: Mezzano Sinistro** {#lesson-4:-mezzano-sinistro}

**The Left Horizontal Cut**

## **Physical Description** {#physical-description-3}

**Path of the Blade:**

* Travels horizontally from left to right  
* Mirror image of mezzano (destro)  
* Same height options (high, middle, low)

**Body Mechanics:**

* Hip rotation from left to right  
* Mirror mechanics of right horizontal cut

## **Tactical Function** {#tactical-function-3}

**Combination Striking:** Mezzano cuts work well in continuous rotation — right to left to right again

**Deception:** Horizontal cuts from unexpected angles (especially from Fenestra guards) are difficult to read

## **Drill 1: Continuous Rotation** {#drill-1:-continuous-rotation}

**Solo:**

1. Fenestra Destra → Mezzano (destro) → Fenestra Sinestra  
2. Fenestra Sinestra → Mezzano sinistro → Fenestra Destra  
3. Continue for 20 rotations (10 each direction)

**Goal:** Smooth, continuous rotation without stopping

# **Lesson 5: Sottano (Destro)** {#lesson-5:-sottano-(destro)}

**The Right Rising Cut**

## **Physical Description** {#physical-description-4}

**Path of the Blade:**

* Starts low on the right side  
* Travels diagonally upward  
* Cuts from right hip toward opponent's left shoulder/head  
* Opposite trajectory from fendente

**Body Mechanics:**

* Often delivered with explosive upward movement  
* Rising from rear-weighted position (like Coda Longa)  
* Legs drive upward, body extends

**Ending Position:**

* Typically finishes in high guard (Posta Frontale, Donna, or Fenestra)  
* Blade ends high

## **Tactical Function** {#tactical-function-4}

**Primary Targets:**

* Arms (especially if opponent strikes downward)  
* Chin/jaw (rising under their guard)  
* Torso (rising from below)

**Surprise Factor:** Rising cuts are less common and harder to see coming

**Counter-Strike:** Excellent for intercepting descending cuts — you cut their arms as they cut downward

## **Connection to Guards** {#connection-to-guards-2}

**Primary Starting Guards:**

* Coda Longa (rear guard, loaded spring)  
* Dente di Zenghiaro (low position)  
* Porta di Ferro (any variation)

**Ending Guards:**

* Posta Frontale/Corona (high)  
* Posta di Fenestra Destra (high, extended)  
* Posta di Donna Destra (high, chambered)

## **Drill 1: From Coda Longa** {#drill-1:-from-coda-longa}

**Solo:**

1. Adopt Coda Longa (blade behind, rear-weighted)  
2. Explosively step forward with rear foot  
3. Release rising cut (sottano destro)  
4. Finish in Posta Frontale (high guard)  
5. Repeat 10 times

**Key Point:** This should feel like a compressed spring releasing — all the energy stored in the rear position explodes upward

## **Drill 2: Intercepting Descending Cuts** {#drill-2:-intercepting-descending-cuts}

**With Partner (Slow):**

1. Partner B delivers slow fendente (descending cut)  
2. Partner A in low guard (Zenghiaro or Ferro)  
3. A delivers sottano (rising cut) to intercept B's arms  
4. A's rising blade meets B's descending blade  
5. Repeat 10 times, then switch roles

**Tactical Lesson:** Rising cuts can intercept and deflect descending attacks while simultaneously striking the opponent's arms

## **Common Errors** {#common-errors-3}

**Error:** Weak, slow rising cut

**Fix:** Use leg drive and explosive hip extension; this cut needs power

**Error:** Only using arms

**Fix:** The power comes from the legs extending upward

---

# **Lesson 6: Sottano Sinistro** {#lesson-6:-sottano-sinistro}

**The Left Rising Cut**

## **Physical Description** {#physical-description-5}

**Path of the Blade:**

* Starts low on the left side  
* Travels diagonally upward toward opponent's right shoulder/head  
* Mirror image of sottano (destro)

**Body Mechanics:**

* Same explosive rising motion from the left side  
* Mirror mechanics

## **Tactical Function** {#tactical-function-5}

Same as sottano (destro), but from the opposite side. Often used in alternating combination with right sottano.

## **Drill 1: Bilateral Rising Cuts** {#drill-1:-bilateral-rising-cuts}

**Solo:**

1. Low guard (center) → Sottano destro → High guard right  
2. Return to low guard (center) → Sottano sinistro → High guard left  
3. Repeat 10 complete cycles

# **Lesson 7: Punta** {#lesson-7:-punta}

**The Thrust**

## **Physical Description** {#physical-description-6}

**Path of the Blade:**

* Straight line from your position to the target  
* No arc or curve  
* Point leads, traveling along centerline

**Body Mechanics:**

* Extending thrust with step forward  
* Hips drive forward  
* Arms extend fully  
* Weight shifts to front foot

**Ending Position:**

* Posta Longa (extended)  
* Or recovery to Fenestra

## **Tactical Function** {#tactical-function-6}

**Primary Targets:**

* Face/throat (high line)  
* Chest/torso (middle line)  
* Abdomen (low line)  
* Armpit (if arms are raised)

**Speed:** Generally the fastest of the seven blows

**Priority:** Extended thrust occupies centerline first ("point against point, the longer makes offense first")

**Precision:** More precise than cuts; can target specific vulnerabilities

## **Connection to Guards** {#connection-to-guards-3}

**Primary Starting Guards:**

* Posta di Fenestra (both sides) — most natural thrusting position  
* Posta Longa (already extended)  
* Any guard can thrust, but Fenestra is optimized for it

**Ending Guards:**

* Posta Longa (extended point)  
* Can recover to Fenestra

## **Drill 1: Basic Thrusting** {#drill-1:-basic-thrusting}

**Solo:**

1. Posta di Fenestra Destra  
2. Step forward with rear foot  
3. Extend thrust fully  
4. Finish in Posta Longa  
5. Recover to Fenestra  
6. Repeat 20 times

**Focus:**

* Straight line to target  
* Point leads, hands follow  
* Full extension

## **Drill 2: Target Precision** {#drill-2:-target-precision}

**With Partner (Slow, No Contact):**

1. Partner B holds various target positions with their hand  
2. A delivers slow thrust to each target:  
   * High (face)  
   * Middle (chest)  
   * Low (abdomen)  
3. Focus on precise point control  
4. Repeat 5 times at each height  
5. Switch roles

## **Common Errors** {#common-errors-4}

**Error:** Point wandering off target

**Fix:** Focus the eyes on the target; point follows the gaze

**Error:** Bent arms at extension

**Fix:** Fully extend arms; complete the thrust

**Error:** Leaning forward (losing structure)

**Fix:** Step forward, don't lean; maintain upright structure

# **Integration: Combining the Seven Blows** {#integration:-combining-the-seven-blows}

## **Guard Flow with All Seven** {#guard-flow-with-all-seven}

**Solo Drill:**

1. Posta di Donna Destra → Fendente → Zenghiaro  
2. Zenghiaro → Sottano destro → Frontale  
3. Frontale → Fendente → Zenghiaro  
4. Zenghiaro → Flow to Porta di Ferro Mezana  
5. Mezana → Thrust → Longa  
6. Longa → Pull to Fenestra Destra  
7. Fenestra → Mezzano destro → Fenestra Sinestra  
8. Fenestra Sinestra → Thrust → Longa  
9. Longa → Pull to Coda Longa  
10. Coda Longa → Sottano sinistro → Frontale

Repeat the circuit, incorporating all seven blows

## **Tactical Combinations** {#tactical-combinations}

**High-Low Combination:**

* Fendente (high descending) → Opponent raises guard  
* Sottano (low rising) → Strike under their raised arms

**Side-to-Side Combination:**

* Mezzano destro (right horizontal) → Opponent defends right  
* Mezzano sinistro (left horizontal) → Strike opposite side

**Cut-Thrust Combination:**

* Fendente (cut) → Opponent focuses on cutting defense  
* Punta (thrust) → Strike with point while they expect cut

## **Common Tactical Principles** {#common-tactical-principles}

**Opposite Angles:** Follow a descending cut with a rising cut, or vice versa

**Opposite Sides:** Alternate between right and left to prevent defensive patterns

**Mixed Types:** Alternate between cuts and thrusts to create uncertainty

**Tempo:** Vary the rhythm — sometimes fast combinations, sometimes single strikes with pauses

# **The Longsword Guards (Poste)**

---

# Introduction: Why Start with Guards? {#introduction:-why-start-with-guards?}

After understanding Fiore's four virtues (Lynx, Lion, Tiger, Elephant), the natural progression is to learn the **twelve cardinal guards** of the longsword. These are called *poste* in Italian — positions from which you attack, defend, and counter.

Fiore organizes his entire longsword system around these guards. They are not static poses you hold — they are **dynamic positions that connect every movement**. Every cut ends in a guard. Every thrust begins from a guard. Understanding the poste is understanding the structure of the entire art.

---

# Fiore's Three Classifications {#fiore's-three-classifications}

Fiore divides the twelve guards into three tactical types:

**Pulsativa (Pulsing/Beating Guards)** — Guards that can explosively transition from stillness to action

* Posta di Donna (both sides)  
* Porta di Ferro (all three variations)

**Stabile (Stable Guards)** — Guards that provide strong structural defense

* Dente di Zenghiaro (both variations)  
* Posta Breve  
* Bicorno

**Instabile (Mutable/Unstable Guards)** — Guards that emphasize deception and mobility

* Posta di Fenestra (both sides)  
* Posta Longa  
* Coda Longa  
* Posta Frontale/Corona

# **Lesson 1: Posta di Donna Destra** {#lesson-1:-posta-di-donna-destra}

**Classification:** Pulsativa (Pulsing)

## **Getty Manuscript Text** {#getty-manuscript-text}

*"Posta di Donna son e di spada posso fare tuti li .vij. colpi e posso ben fare couerture de tuti colpi. E le altre prese rompo cum li gran colpi che posso fare. E de cambiare de punta io son presta e sempre lo pe de auanti se tore de uia e quello de dredo se mette per trauerso. E zerchadore serà descouerto e questo farà presto e certo."*

**Translation:**

"I am the Woman's Guard and with the sword I can make all seven blows, and I can cover well from all blows. And I break the other guards with the great blows that I can make. And to exchange the thrust I am always ready. The foot which is in front steps off the line, and that which is behind passes across. And the opponent will be unprotected, and this will happen quickly and surely."

## **Physical Description** {#physical-description-7}

\[Getty image here\]

**Body Position:**

* Left shoulder faces the opponent  
* Weight can be forward or rear-weighted (both shown in manuscript)  
* Sword is chambered over the right shoulder  
* Blade wound so far back that the point aims toward the left knee  
* Point height varies but is often held down

**Hand Position:**

* Hands high over right shoulder  
* Rear hand (left) supports at pommel  
* Front hand (right) grips normally

## **Tactical Function** {#tactical-function-7}

**Offense:** "I can make all seven blows" — This guard can deliver any of Fiore's seven strikes (fendente, mezzano, sottano from both sides, plus the thrust)

**Defense:** "I can cover well from all blows" — The chambered position allows you to intercept incoming attacks

**Breaking Guards:** "I break the other guards with the great blows" — The extreme wind-up generates tremendous momentum, making your cuts difficult to stop

**Against Thrusts:** "To exchange the thrust I am always ready" — You can step offline and deliver a powerful descending blow while the thrust passes harmlessly

## **Connection to the Four Virtues** {#connection-to-the-four-virtues-1}

* **Elephant (Fortitude):** Rear-weighted stance moves your body out of range while maintaining strong structure  
* **Tiger (Celerity):** The long wind-up trades some speed for maximum momentum — understanding this trade-off is key  
* **Lynx (Prudence):** Requires accurate distance judgment since the blade travels far to reach the opponent  
* **Lion (Audacity):** Commits fully to powerful, decisive strikes

## **Drill 1: Basic Structure** {#drill-1:-basic-structure}

**Solo Practice:**

1. Adopt left foot forward stance  
2. Chamber sword over right shoulder (point toward left knee)  
3. Hold for 10 seconds, checking: shoulder blade engagement, hip position, weight distribution  
4. Repeat with rear-weighted variant  
5. Repeat with forward-weighted variant

**Partner Practice:**

1. Partner A assumes Posta di Donna  
2. Partner B walks around at various distances and angles  
3. A maintains the guard structure, pivoting to track B  
4. Switch roles

## **Drill 2: The Great Blow (Fendente)** {#drill-2:-the-great-blow-(fendente)}

**The Cut:**

From Posta di Donna, the natural strike is a powerful descending diagonal cut (fendente). The blade travels in a long arc, building momentum.

**Solo:**

1. Posta di Donna (right)  
2. Step forward with right foot, releasing the cut  
3. Let the blade travel its full arc  
4. Finish in Dente di Zenghiaro (low guard) — we'll learn this next lesson  
5. Repeat 10 times, focusing on smooth acceleration

**Partner (Slow):**

1. Partner A in Posta di Donna  
2. Partner B in any guard  
3. A delivers slow-motion fendente  
4. B observes the path of the blade  
5. Switch roles

## **Drill 3: Off-Line Footwork** {#drill-3:-off-line-footwork}

Fiore says: "The foot which is in front steps off the line, and that which is behind passes across."

This describes a **passing step with an angle**:

**Solo:**

1. Start in Posta di Donna, left foot forward  
2. Front (left) foot steps offline (to the left) at 45 degrees  
3. Rear (right) foot passes through to become the new front foot  
4. You've moved forward and to the side simultaneously  
5. Practice 10 times without the sword  
6. Practice 10 times with the fendente

## **Common Errors** {#common-errors-5}

**Error:** Holding the sword too close to the body

**Fix:** Wind the blade further back — point should aim at your left knee or beyond

**Error:** Dropping the rear hand

**Fix:** Both hands stay high; the pommel should be near your ear

**Error:** Flat feet

**Fix:** Stay on the balls of your feet; maintain mobility

# **Lesson 2: Posta di Fenestra Destra** {#lesson-2:-posta-di-fenestra-destra}

**Classification:** Instabile (Mutable)

## **Getty Manuscript Text** {#getty-manuscript-text-1}

*"Posta de fenestra son, che de malitie e de inganni sempre me presto. E de couerture e de feridure son magistra. E a tute prese io facio question cum le soprane e le tere\[ne\]. E dall' una a l'altra io uago spesso per enganar lo compagno. E grande punte metto e de rompere e de cambiare so ben fare questi zoghi."*

**Translation:**

"I am the Window Guard, and I am always ready with skill and deception. And I am a master of covering and wounding. And I question all the guards, both the high and the low. And from one to another I often wander to deceive the companion. And I place great thrusts and I know well how to break and exchange these plays."

## **Physical Description** {#physical-description-8}

\[**INSERT GETTY IMAGE: Folio 23v**\]

**Body Position:**

* Rear-weighted stance, body pulled back  
* Sword held on the right side, point forward  
* True (front) edge faces up  
* Hands approximately head height  
* Point threatening the opponent's face/throat

**Tactical Note:** The rear weight keeps your body at maximum distance while the point remains threatening.

## **Tactical Function** {#tactical-function-8}

**Deception:** "I am always ready with skill and deception" and "from one to another I often wander to deceive"

**Versatility:** "Master of covering and wounding" — equally effective for offense and defense

**Universal Application:** "I question all the guards, both the high and the low" — Can oppose any guard

**Strong Thrusts:** "I place great thrusts" — The extended point position makes thrusting natural and powerful

## **The "Tasting" or "Questioning" Concept** {#the-"tasting"-or-"questioning"-concept}

Fiore uses the word "question" (*facio question*) — this means **testing or provoking your opponent**. From Fenestra, you:

* Threaten with the point to draw a response  
* Feint to one opening to attack another  
* Move between guards to create confusion  
* Force your opponent to commit first

This is the **Lynx virtue** in action, observation, reading the opponent, forcing them to reveal their intentions.

## **Drill 1: Structure and Mobility** {#drill-1:-structure-and-mobility}

**Solo:**

1. Adopt rear-weighted stance  
2. Bring sword to right side, true edge up, point forward  
3. Hands at head height  
4. Hold for 10 seconds  
5. **Without moving your feet**, shift to Posta di Donna  
6. **Without moving your feet**, return to Fenestra  
7. Repeat 10 times

This teaches you that Fenestra and Donna are **closely related** and you can flow between them.

**Partner:**

1. A in Fenestra  
2. B walks around at various distances  
3. A pivots to keep point threatening B's face  
4. A should maintain rear weight, keeping body back

## **Drill 2: The Thrust** {#drill-2:-the-thrust}

**Solo:**

1. Posta di Fenestra  
2. Extend the thrust (step forward with rear foot)  
3. Finish in Posta Longa (we'll learn this in Lesson 4\)  
4. Repeat 10 times

**Partner (Slow, No Contact):**

1. Partner A in Fenestra  
2. Partner B in any guard  
3. A delivers slow thrust toward B's face  
4. B observes (does not block)  
5. Switch roles

## **Drill 3: Guard Transitions (Deception)** {#drill-3:-guard-transitions-(deception)}

Fiore says: "From one to another I often wander to deceive the companion."

**Solo:**

1. Fenestra Destra (right window)  
2. Flow to Posta di Donna Destra  
3. Flow to Coda Longa (rear guard, we'll learn this later)  
4. Flow back to Fenestra  
5. Practice the circuit 10 times, smooth and continuous

**Partner:**

1. A in Fenestra  
2. B in any guard  
3. A transitions between guards (Fenestra → Donna → Fenestra)  
4. B reacts to the changes  
5. No strikes yet, just positional pressure

## **Common Errors** {#common-errors-6}

**Error:** Weight too far forward

**Fix:** Pull hips and shoulders back; point threatens but body stays safe

**Error:** Point dropping

**Fix:** Keep hands at head height; point should threaten the face

**Error:** Stiff arms

**Fix:** Slight bend in elbows; maintain fluidity

#  **Lesson 3: Dente di Zenghiaro / Tutta Porta di Ferro** {#lesson-3:-dente-di-zenghiaro-/-tutta-porta-di-ferro}

**Classification:** Stabile (Stable) / Pulsativa (Pulsing)

## **Getty Manuscript Text** {#getty-manuscript-text-2}

*"Tuta porta di ferro son e sto in grande forteza e per aspectar ogni arma da mane bona son io e corta se io ho bona spada si non troppo longa. E partome cum couertura e uago a strenger punte cambio e la mia metto dentro, ancho rebato le punte in tera e sempro uago cum uno passo e de ogni colpo facio couertura."*

**Translation:**

"I am Full Iron Door and I stand in great strength. I am good for waiting for every hand weapon, long and short, if I have a good sword, not one that is too long. And I leave with a cover and I go to close. I exchange the thrusts and put mine in. Also, I beat the thrusts to the ground, and I always go with a pass \[passing step\], and I make a cover of every blow."

## **Physical Description** {#physical-description-9}

\[**INSERT GETTY IMAGE: Folio 22v**\]

**Body Position:**

* Forward-weighted stance  
* Sword held low, point forward and upward  
* Hands near the hip  
* Point threatens the opponent  
* Strong, grounded posture

**The Name:** "Boar's Tooth" (*Dente di Zenghiaro*) refers to the upward-pointing blade, like a boar's tusk. "Full Iron Door" (*Tutta Porta di Ferro*) emphasizes the defensive strength.

## **Tactical Function** {#tactical-function-9}

**Defensive Strength:** "I stand in great strength", This is the **Elephant virtue** embodied in a guard

**Universal Defense:** "Good for waiting for every hand weapon", Effective against all attacks

**Thrust Beating:** "I beat the thrusts to the ground", The low position allows you to parry thrusts downward

**Closing Distance:** "I go to close", This guard is excellent for entering close range (narrow play)

**Covering:** "I make a cover of every blow", Every action from this guard includes a defensive element

## **The Elephant Connection** {#the-elephant-connection}

Remember the Elephant from the segno:

"I am the elephant and I have a castle for a burden. And never do I kneel down nor do I lose my true place."

Dente di Zenghiaro is the **castle on the elephant's back** — a stable, defensive position that cannot be easily moved. Your structure is strong and grounded.

## **Drill 1: Structure and Stability** {#drill-1:-structure-and-stability}

**Solo:**

1. Forward-weighted stance  
2. Bring sword to low position, point forward and up  
3. Hands near right hip  
4. Have a partner gently push your shoulders, arms, and torso  
5. Maintain your structure without collapsing or being moved  
6. This is "finding your Elephant"

**Partner:**

1. A in Dente di Zenghiaro  
2. B delivers slow, light pressure with their sword  
3. A maintains structure, doesn't retreat  
4. Switch roles

## **Drill 2: Beating the Thrust** {#drill-2:-beating-the-thrust}

Fiore says: "I beat the thrusts to the ground."

**Partner (Slow):**

1. A in Dente di Zenghiaro  
2. B delivers slow thrust from Posta Longa (high or middle line)  
3. A uses rising motion to deflect thrust downward  
4. A steps forward as they deflect ("I always go with a pass")  
5. Repeat 10 times each role

**Key Point:** You don't retreat. You deflect *and* advance simultaneously.

## **Drill 3: Finishing Position** {#drill-3:-finishing-position}

Remember from Posta di Donna: "The great blow finishes in Dente di Zenghiaro."

**Solo:**

1. Posta di Donna (right)  
2. Deliver fendente (descending cut)  
3. Let the blade travel its full arc  
4. Finish in Dente di Zenghiaro (low position, point forward)  
5. Repeat 10 times

This teaches you that **guards are connected**. Strikes begin in one guard and end in another.

## **Common Errors** {#common-errors-7}

**Error:** Point too low (aiming at the ground)

**Fix:** Point should angle upward, threatening the opponent

**Error:** Retreating when pressured

**Fix:** Remember the Elephant — you don't kneel, you don't lose your footing

**Error:** Hands too far from the body

**Fix:** Hands near the hip create a strong structure

# **Lesson 4: Posta Longa** {#lesson-4:-posta-longa}

**Classification:** Instabile (Mutable)

## **Getty Manuscript Text** {#getty-manuscript-text-3}

*"Io son posta longa e achosi te aspetto. E in la presa che tu mi uoray fare, Lo mio brazo dritto che sta in erto, Sotto lo tuo stancho lo mettero per certo. E di subito te feriro cum una punta."*

**Translation:**

"I am Long Guard and like this I wait for you. And in the grip/technique that you would like to make against me, my right arm which stands raised, I will place it under your left for certain. And suddenly I will wound you with a thrust."

## **Physical Description** {#physical-description-10}

\[**INSERT GETTY IMAGE: Folio 22r**\]

**Body Position:**

* Extended stance, point directly threatening opponent  
* Hands approximately solar plexus height  
* Sword fully extended  
* Weight can vary (forward or rear)  
* Point occupies the centerline

**The Extended Threat:** Posta Longa **occupies space**. Your point is already at the opponent, forcing them to deal with it before they can attack you.

## **Tactical Function** {#tactical-function-10}

**Waiting Game:** "Like this I wait for you" — This is a patient guard; you force the opponent to commit first

**Priority:** Because your point is extended, you have **first strike advantage**. Fiore notes that "point against point, the longer makes offense first."

**Deception:** Despite appearing committed, Posta Longa is classified as *Instabile* (mutable). You can quickly transition to other guards.

**Binding:** "I will place my right arm under your left" — This refers to controlling the opponent's sword with your hands/arms (the presa, or grip)

## **Drill 1: The Extended Threat** {#drill-1:-the-extended-threat}

**Partner:**

1. A in Posta Longa  
2. B in Posta di Donna (or any other guard)  
3. B attempts to advance and strike  
4. A maintains the extended point, forcing B to deal with the threat  
5. B must either: deflect the point, step around it, or withdraw  
6. Switch roles

**Teaching Point:** Explain that the extended point creates a **tactical problem** your opponent must solve.

## **Drill 2: Point Against Point** {#drill-2:-point-against-point}

**Partner:**

1. Both partners in Posta Longa, facing each other  
2. Slowly close distance  
3. Whoever has the longer reach (or extends more fully) threatens first  
4. Practice finding this "line of threat"

Fiore says: "Point against point, the longer makes offense first."

## **Drill 3: Thrust and Return** {#drill-3:-thrust-and-return}

**Solo:**

1. Posta di Fenestra (right)  
2. Extend thrust, finishing in Posta Longa  
3. Hold Posta Longa for 2 seconds  
4. Recover to Fenestra  
5. Repeat 10 times

This teaches the relationship between Fenestra (chambered threat) and Longa (extended threat).

## **Common Errors** {#common-errors-8}

**Error:** Overextending (leaning forward, off balance)

**Fix:** Extend the sword, not your body; maintain structure

**Error:** Point wandering off centerline

**Fix:** Keep point threatening the face/throat

**Error:** Locked elbows

**Fix:** Slight bend in elbows; stay dynamic

# **Lesson 5: Posta di Donna La Sinestra** {#lesson-5:-posta-di-donna-la-sinestra}

**Classification:** Pulsativa (Pulsing)

## **Getty Manuscript Text** {#getty-manuscript-text-4}

The Getty manuscript does not provide separate text for the left-side guards, as they are mirror images of their right-side counterparts. The tactical principles remain the same, but now applied from the opposite side.

## **Physical Description** {#physical-description-11}

\[**INSERT GETTY IMAGE: Left-side Donna**\]

**Body Position:**

* **Right shoulder** faces the opponent (mirror of Lesson 1\)  
* Weight can be forward or rear-weighted  
* Sword is chambered over the **left shoulder**  
* Blade wound so far back that the point aims toward the **right knee**  
* Point height varies but is often held down

**Hand Position:**

* Hands high over left shoulder  
* Rear hand (right) supports at pommel  
* Front hand (left) grips normally

## **Tactical Function** {#tactical-function-11}

All tactical functions from Posta di Donna Destra apply:

**Offense:** Can deliver all seven blows from the left side

**Defense:** Chambered position allows interception of incoming attacks

**Breaking Guards:** Extreme wind-up generates tremendous momentum

**Against Thrusts:** Step offline to the right and deliver a powerful descending blow

## **Why Learn Both Sides?** {#why-learn-both-sides?}

**Tactical Versatility:** Being able to strike from both sides makes you unpredictable. Your opponent must defend against threats from either direction.

**Guard Flow:** Many of Fiore's plays involve transitioning between right and left guards. You cannot execute these without proficiency on both sides.

**Historical Evidence:** The Getty manuscript shows plays from both sides, indicating Fiore expected practitioners to train both.

**Completeness:** A complete martial artist can fight equally well from either side.

## **Connection to the Four Virtues** {#connection-to-the-four-virtues-2}

* **Elephant (Fortitude):** Maintaining strong structure is even more important when working from your non-dominant side  
* **Tiger (Celerity):** Develop equal speed on both sides through deliberate practice  
* **Lynx (Prudence):** Distance judgment must be recalibrated for the opposite side  
* **Lion (Audacity):** Requires courage to commit fully from your weaker side

## **Drill 1: Basic Structure (Left Side)** {#drill-1:-basic-structure-(left-side)}

**Solo Practice:**

1. Adopt **right foot forward** stance  
2. Chamber sword over **left shoulder** (point toward right knee)  
3. Hold for 10 seconds, checking: shoulder blade engagement, hip position, weight distribution  
4. Repeat with rear-weighted variant  
5. Repeat with forward-weighted variant  
6. Compare the feeling to right-side Donna — what feels different?

**Partner Practice:**

1. Partner A assumes Posta di Donna La Sinestra  
2. Partner B walks around at various distances and angles  
3. A maintains the guard structure, pivoting to track B  
4. Switch roles

**Asymmetry Drill:**

1. Adopt Posta di Donna Destra (right)  
2. Hold for 5 seconds  
3. Flow to Posta di Donna La Sinestra (left)  
4. Hold for 5 seconds  
5. Repeat 10 times, noting differences in comfort and stability

## **Drill 2: The Great Blow from the Left (Fendente Sinistro)** {#drill-2:-the-great-blow-from-the-left-(fendente-sinistro)}

**The Cut:**

From Posta di Donna La Sinestra, deliver a powerful descending diagonal cut from the left side. The blade travels in a long arc, building momentum.

**Solo:**

1. Posta di Donna La Sinestra (left)  
2. Step forward with left foot, releasing the cut  
3. Let the blade travel its full arc  
4. Finish in Dente di Zenghiaro (low guard)  
5. Repeat 10 times, focusing on smooth acceleration

**Partner (Slow):**

1. Partner A in Posta di Donna La Sinestra  
2. Partner B in any guard  
3. A delivers slow-motion fendente from the left  
4. B observes the path of the blade and notes how it differs from the right-side cut  
5. Switch roles

## **Drill 3: Alternating Sides** {#drill-3:-alternating-sides}

**Solo:**

1. Start in Posta di Donna Destra (right)  
2. Deliver fendente, finishing in Dente di Zenghiaro  
3. Flow to Posta di Donna La Sinestra (left)  
4. Deliver fendente from the left, finishing in Dente di Zenghiaro  
5. Repeat the circuit 10 times

This drill teaches you to flow between sides seamlessly, building ambidexterity.

**Partner:**

1. A in Posta di Donna Destra  
2. B in any guard  
3. A delivers fendente from the right  
4. A immediately flows to Posta di Donna La Sinestra  
5. A delivers fendente from the left  
6. B must defend against strikes from both sides  
7. Switch roles

## **Common Errors** {#common-errors-9}

**Error:** Weaker commitment from non-dominant side

**Fix:** Focus on full extension and complete follow-through; don't pull the strike

**Error:** Dropping the guard too quickly after the strike

**Fix:** Maintain structure all the way through the recovery to Dente di Zenghiaro

**Error:** Telegraphing the side switch

**Fix:** Keep transitions smooth and continuous; don't pause between sides

**Error:** Trying to compensate for weakness with excessive force

**Fix:** Focus on technique and structure rather than power; speed and power will come with practice

## **Training Notes for Instructors** {#training-notes-for-instructors}

**Expect Asymmetry:** Most students will show significant differences between their dominant and non-dominant sides. This is normal.

**Gradual Progress:** Build proficiency on the left side gradually. Don't rush students into sparring from their weak side before they're ready.

**Equal Practice Time:** Encourage students to practice both sides equally, even though the non-dominant side will feel awkward at first.

**Positive Reinforcement:** Celebrate improvement on the weaker side. Even small gains represent significant neurological development.

# **Lesson 6: Posta di Fenestra La Sinestra** {#lesson-6:-posta-di-fenestra-la-sinestra}

**Classification:** Instabile (Mutable)

## **Getty Manuscript Text** {#getty-manuscript-text-5}

As with Lesson 5, the Getty manuscript does not provide separate text for the left-side guards. The tactical principles from Posta di Fenestra Destra apply, mirrored to the left side.

## **Physical Description** {#physical-description-12}

\[**INSERT GETTY IMAGE: Left-side Fenestra**\]

**Body Position:**

* Rear-weighted stance, body pulled back  
* Sword held on the **left side**, point forward  
* True (front) edge faces up  
* Hands approximately head height  
* Point threatening the opponent's face/throat

**Tactical Note:** The rear weight keeps your body at maximum distance while the point remains threatening.

## **Tactical Function** {#tactical-function-12}

All tactical functions from Posta di Fenestra Destra apply from the left side:

**Deception:** "From one to another I often wander to deceive" — now you can wander between four guards (Donna and Fenestra, both right and left)

**Versatility:** Equally effective for offense and defense from either side

**Universal Application:** Can oppose any guard from the left side

**Strong Thrusts:** The extended point position makes thrusting natural and powerful

## **The Expanded "Questioning" Concept** {#the-expanded-"questioning"-concept}

With both Fenestra Destra and Fenestra La Sinestra available, you can now:

* Threaten from one side, then switch to the other  
* Force your opponent to defend multiple lines simultaneously  
* Create openings by drawing their attention to one side while attacking from the other  
* Flow continuously between left and right, never giving them a settled target

This multiplies the deceptive potential that Fiore describes.

## **Connection to the Four Virtues** {#connection-to-the-four-virtues-3}

* **Lynx (Prudence):** Observing which side your opponent is weaker at defending  
* **Lion (Audacity):** Committing to attacks from either side with equal confidence  
* **Tiger (Celerity):** Switching sides rapidly to create confusion  
* **Elephant (Fortitude):** Maintaining stable structure regardless of which side you're on

## **Drill 1: Structure and Mobility (Left Side)** {#drill-1:-structure-and-mobility-(left-side)}

**Solo:**

1. Adopt rear-weighted stance  
2. Bring sword to **left side**, true edge up, point forward  
3. Hands at head height  
4. Hold for 10 seconds  
5. **Without moving your feet**, shift to Posta di Donna La Sinestra  
6. **Without moving your feet**, return to Fenestra La Sinestra  
7. Repeat 10 times

**Partner:**

1. A in Fenestra La Sinestra  
2. B walks around at various distances  
3. A pivots to keep point threatening B's face  
4. A should maintain rear weight, keeping body back  
5. Switch roles

## **Drill 2: The Thrust from the Left** {#drill-2:-the-thrust-from-the-left}

**Solo:**

1. Posta di Fenestra La Sinestra  
2. Extend the thrust (step forward with rear foot)  
3. Finish in Posta Longa  
4. Repeat 10 times

**Partner (Slow, No Contact):**

1. Partner A in Fenestra La Sinestra  
2. Partner B in any guard  
3. A delivers slow thrust toward B's face from the left side  
4. B observes (does not block)  
5. Switch roles

## **Drill 3: Four-Corner Guard Flow** {#drill-3:-four-corner-guard-flow}

This drill integrates all four guards learned so far: Donna Destra, Fenestra Destra, Donna La Sinestra, Fenestra La Sinestra.

**Solo:**

1. Posta di Donna Destra (right, high, chambered)  
2. Flow to Posta di Fenestra Destra (right, high, extended)  
3. Flow to Posta di Donna La Sinestra (left, high, chambered)  
4. Flow to Posta di Fenestra La Sinestra (left, high, extended)  
5. Complete the circuit 10 times smoothly

**The Goal:** Move between all four high guards fluidly. Your opponent should not be able to predict which side or which type (chambered vs extended) you'll use next.

**Partner:**

1. A cycles through all four high guards (Donna Destra → Fenestra Destra → Donna La Sinestra → Fenestra La Sinestra)  
2. B stands at various distances and angles  
3. A must keep the appropriate threat active from each guard  
4. B calls out which guard A is in; A confirms or corrects  
5. Switch roles

## **Drill 4: Side-Switching Deception** {#drill-4:-side-switching-deception}

**Partner (Light Contact):**

1. A in Fenestra Destra (right side, threatening)  
2. B in any guard, defending against the right-side threat  
3. A quickly switches to Fenestra La Sinestra (left side)  
4. B must adjust defense to the new threat angle  
5. A can deliver a light thrust if B is slow to adjust  
6. Repeat 10 times, gradually increasing speed  
7. Switch roles

This drill teaches the core deceptive principle of Fenestra: "from one to another I often wander to deceive the companion."

## **Common Errors** {#common-errors-10}

**Error:** Point dropping during side transition

**Fix:** Keep hands at head height throughout the entire transition; the point should never lose its threat

**Error:** Shifting weight forward during the switch

**Fix:** Maintain rear weight; stay at maximum distance while threatening

**Error:** Obvious wind-up before switching sides

**Fix:** Make the transition smooth and continuous; don't telegraph your intention

**Error:** Losing structure when working from the non-dominant side

**Fix:** Slow down and focus on proper form; speed will come later

## **Advanced Application: The Cross-Guard Matrix** {#advanced-application:-the-cross-guard-matrix}

With Donna and Fenestra available on both sides, you now have a 2x2 matrix of high guards:

|  | Right | Left |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |
| **Chambered** | Donna Destra | Donna La Sinestra |
| **Extended** | Fenestra Destra | Fenestra La Sinestra |

**Strategic Principle:** You can move to any guard in this matrix from any other guard. This creates enormous tactical flexibility.

Example Sequence:

1. Start in Fenestra Destra (right, extended, threatening)  
2. Opponent commits to defend the right side  
3. Switch to Donna La Sinestra (left, chambered, loading)  
4. Opponent must now defend against a left-side power cut  
5. Before the cut arrives, switch to Fenestra La Sinestra (left, extended, thrust)  
6. Opponent is caught between defending the cut and the thrust

This level of deception is what Fiore means by "master of covering and wounding" and "I question all the guards."

## **Training Notes for Instructors** {#training-notes-for-instructors-1}

**Progressive Complexity:** Don't introduce the cross-guard matrix concept until students are comfortable with each individual guard.

**Both Sides Matter:** Emphasize that the left-side guards are not "backup" guards — they are essential parts of the complete system.

**Flow Over Power:** When learning side switches, prioritize smooth transitions over powerful strikes.

**Deception Practice:** Create drills where students must genuinely deceive their partners, not just go through motions.

# **Lesson 7: Coda Longa (Long Tail)** {#lesson-7:-coda-longa-(long-tail)}

**Classification:** Instabile (Mutable)

## **Getty Manuscript Text** {#getty-manuscript-text-6}

*"Coda longa son, e son posta falsa, e per zerca strenzer son messa. E zo che paro de voler defendre, certamente io uoglio offendere."*

**Translation:**

"I am Long Tail, and I am a false guard, and I am set to seek and close. And that which I appear to want to defend, certainly I want to offend."

## **Physical Description** {#physical-description-13}

\[**INSERT GETTY IMAGE: Folio 24r**\]

**Body Position:**

* Rear-weighted stance, body pulled back defensively  
* Sword held behind the body, point backward and down  
* Right foot forward, left foot back  
* Blade trails behind like a tail  
* Appears highly defensive or even retreating

**Hand Position:**

* Hands low, near the hip or behind the hip  
* Rear hand extended back with the pommel  
* Sword points toward your own rear

**The Name:** "Long Tail" refers to the blade extending far behind you, like an animal's tail. The guard appears passive, even vulnerable.

## **Tactical Function** {#tactical-function-13}

**Deception:** "I am a false guard" — This is Fiore's most deceptive guard. You appear to be retreating or defending, but you're actually loading for a powerful attack.

**Closing Distance:** "I am set to seek and close" — Despite appearing defensive, this guard is designed for aggressive forward movement.

**Surprise Attack:** "That which I appear to want to defend, certainly I want to offend" — Your opponent thinks you're on the defensive, but you're actually preparing to strike.

**Drawing the Attack:** The withdrawn, defensive posture invites your opponent to attack, giving you an opening.

## **The False Guard Concept** {#the-false-guard-concept}

A "false guard" (*posta falsa*) deliberately misleads your opponent about your intentions. Coda Longa looks like:

* You're exhausted or wounded  
* You're afraid and backing away  
* You're unprepared to strike  
* You've given up the initiative

In reality:

* The blade is loaded like a spring  
* You're perfectly balanced to explode forward  
* You have maximum reach available  
* You control the tempo by baiting the attack

## **Connection to the Four Virtues** {#connection-to-the-four-virtues-4}

* **Lynx (Prudence):** The ultimate expression of the lynx — observing, measuring, baiting the opponent into revealing their intention  
* **Tiger (Celerity):** The extreme chamber allows for explosive speed when you release  
* **Elephant (Fortitude):** Rear weight provides rock-solid stability and safety  
* **Lion (Audacity):** Requires courage to adopt such an apparently vulnerable position

## **Drill 1: Basic Structure** {#drill-1:-basic-structure-1}

**Solo Practice:**

1. Adopt rear-weighted stance, right foot forward  
2. Bring sword behind your body, point trailing backward  
3. Hands low, near or behind the hip  
4. Hold for 10 seconds  
5. Feel the "loaded spring" quality — ready to explode forward  
6. Repeat 10 times

**Partner Practice:**

1. Partner A assumes Coda Longa  
2. Partner B observes from different angles  
3. B describes what A's posture communicates (defensive? tired? retreating?)  
4. A maintains the guard while B circles  
5. Switch roles

## **Drill 2: The Explosive Strike (Sottano)** {#drill-2:-the-explosive-strike-(sottano)}

From Coda Longa, the natural strike is a rising cut from below (*sottano* or *colpo sotano*).

**The Cut:**

The blade whips forward and upward from behind your body, traveling in a long arc. This generates tremendous momentum and is very difficult for the opponent to see coming.

**Solo:**

1. Coda Longa (blade behind you)  
2. Step forward explosively with the rear (left) foot  
3. Release the rising cut, blade traveling from low to high  
4. Finish in Posta Frontale or Posta di Fenestra (high guards)  
5. Repeat 10 times, focusing on the explosive transition

**Partner (Slow):**

1. Partner A in Coda Longa  
2. Partner B in Posta di Donna or Fenestra (high threat)  
3. A delivers slow-motion sottano (rising cut)  
4. B observes how difficult it is to see the strike coming  
5. Switch roles

**Key Point:** The strike should feel like a compressed spring releasing. All the energy stored in the rear position explodes forward in one motion.

## **Drill 3: Baiting the Attack** {#drill-3:-baiting-the-attack}

**Partner (Light Contact):**

1. A adopts Coda Longa  
2. B in any aggressive guard (Donna, Fenestra, Longa)  
3. A appears vulnerable and defensive  
4. B advances to strike  
5. As B commits, A explodes forward with sottano  
6. A's goal: land the rising strike while B is in motion  
7. Repeat 10 times, then switch roles

**Teaching Point:** Explain that Coda Longa is a "trap" guard. You're not actually retreating — you're inviting the opponent to over-commit.

## **Common Errors** {#common-errors-11}

**Error:** Actually being defensive in Coda Longa

**Fix:** Remember this is a false guard — you're not retreating, you're loading for an attack

**Error:** Telegraphing the strike by tensing up

**Fix:** Stay relaxed and loose until the moment you explode forward

**Error:** Insufficient rear weight

**Fix:** Pull the body back fully; the more withdrawn you are, the more explosive potential you have

**Very Strong:** "I am in a very strong guard" — The compact structure provides exceptional defensive strength.

**Quick Strike:** "I want to strike quickly forward" — Despite being defensive, Breve can deliver fast strikes.

## **Drill 2: The Quick Strike** {#drill-2:-the-quick-strike}

**Solo:**

1. Posta Breve (high, compact)  
2. Step forward with front foot  
3. Deliver fast fendente (short, sharp cut)  
4. Immediately return to Posta Breve  
5. Repeat 10 times, emphasizing the quick "out and back" motion

## **Common Errors** {#common-errors-12}

**Error:** Holding the sword too far from the body

**Error:** Over-committing to strikes

**Fix:** Remember Fiore's instruction: strike quickly, then immediately cover

# **Lesson 9: Porta di Ferro Mezana (Middle Iron Door)** {#lesson-9:-porta-di-ferro-mezana-(middle-iron-door)}

## **Getty Manuscript Text** {#getty-manuscript-text-8}

*"Porta di ferro mezana son, e son la uerissima colona, che son in mezo tra l'una e l'altra porta, e per auer modo posso far ogni cosa."*

**Translation:**

"I am Middle Iron Door, and I am the truest column, for I am in the middle between one door and the other, and because of my position I can do all things."

## **Physical Description** {#physical-description-15}

\[**INSERT GETTY IMAGE: Folio 26r**\]

**Body Position:**

* Neutral or slightly forward-weighted stance  
* Sword held at middle height (between high and low)  
* Point forward, threatening the center line  
* Hands approximately at chest or solar plexus height  
* Balanced, centered posture

**Hand Position:**

* Hands at mid-height, neither high nor low  
* Comfortable, natural grip  
* Ready to move in any direction

**The Name:** "Middle Iron Door" (*Porta di Ferro Mezana*) positions the guard between the high guards (Donna, Fenestra) and the low guard (Dente di Zenghiaro / Tutta Porta di Ferro).

## **Tactical Function** {#tactical-function-15}

**The Truest Column:** "I am the truest column" — Fiore describes this as the most reliable, foundational guard.

**In the Middle:** "I am in the middle between one door and the other" — This refers to being positioned between Tutta Porta di Ferro (low) and the other guards.

**Universal Capability:** "Because of my position I can do all things" — The middle position allows maximum versatility.

**Centerline Control:** The point threatens the opponent's centerline, forcing them to deal with it.

## **Connection to the Four Virtues** {#connection-to-the-four-virtues-6}

* **Elephant (Fortitude):** Like a column, this guard provides stable structure  
* **Tiger (Celerity):** The middle position allows fast transition in any direction  
* **Lynx (Prudence):** The balanced position supports careful observation and measurement  
* **Lion (Audacity):** "I can do all things" — you must have the courage to execute any action

## **Drill 1: Basic Structure and Centerline Control** {#drill-1:-basic-structure-and-centerline-control}

**Solo Practice:**

1. Adopt neutral stance  
2. Bring sword to middle height, point forward  
3. Hands at chest/solar plexus height  
4. Point threatening the centerline  
5. Hold for 10 seconds, feeling the balanced, ready position  
6. Repeat 10 times

**Partner Practice:**

1. Partner A in Porta di Ferro Mezana  
2. Partner B attempts to approach from various angles  
3. A pivots to keep the point on the centerline, threatening B  
4. B should feel constantly threatened by A's point  
5. Switch roles

## **Drill 2: Transitioning to Any Guard** {#drill-2:-transitioning-to-any-guard}

Fiore says "I can do all things" — this drill proves it.

**Solo:**

1. Start in Porta di Ferro Mezana (middle, center)  
2. Flow to Posta di Donna (high, right, chambered)  
3. Return to Mezana  
4. Flow to Posta di Fenestra (high, right, extended)  
5. Return to Mezana  
6. Flow to Dente di Zenghiaro (low, stable)  
7. Return to Mezana  
8. Flow to Coda Longa (rear, false)  
9. Return to Mezana  
10. Repeat the circuit, noting how easily Mezana transitions to each position

**Teaching Point:** Porta di Ferro Mezana is a "hub" guard. You can reach any other guard quickly from this position.

## **Common Errors** {#common-errors-13}

**Error:** Dropping the point

**Fix:** Keep the point forward, threatening the centerline at all times

**Error:** Standing too upright or rigid

**Fix:** Adopt an athletic, balanced stance; stay mobile

**Error:** Treating Mezana as passive

**Fix:** This is an active, threatening guard; the point should constantly menace the opponent

# **Lesson 10: Bicorno (Two-Horned Guard)** {#lesson-10:-bicorno-(two-horned-guard)}

**Classification:** Stabile (Stable)

## **Getty Manuscript Text** {#getty-manuscript-text-9}

*"Bicorno son chiamado, per nome si perfecta, che cum doe punte e cum doe tagli, facio mia defesa perfecta. E posso ferir e defender cum li agudissimi mei corni. E per tal cason io son contra le altre prese ad ogne homo adorni."*

**Translation:**

"I am called Two-Horned, by name so perfect, for with two points and with two edges, I make my defense perfect. And I can strike and defend with my very sharp horns. And for this reason I am against the other grips, adorned for every man."

## **Physical Description** {#physical-description-16}

\[**INSERT GETTY IMAGE: Folio 27r**\]

**Body Position:**

* Forward-weighted stance  
* Sword held overhead, approximately horizontal  
* Both hands grip the sword at different points  
* One hand on the handle, one hand on the blade (half-swording grip)  
* Point aims forward from above the head  
* Resembles a bull with horns lowered for charging

**Hand Position:**

* Rear hand on the handle (normal grip)  
* Front hand grips the blade (typically halfway up)  
* Arms form a triangle or "V" shape  
* Blade positioned like two horns extending forward

**The Name:** "Two-Horned" (*Bicorno*) refers to the dual-threat posture: the point threatens like one horn, while the crossguard and pommel threaten like another horn. The guard resembles a charging bull.

## **Tactical Function** {#tactical-function-16}

**Perfect Defense:** "With two points and with two edges, I make my defense perfect" — This guard provides comprehensive protection from above.

**Dual Threats:** "I can strike and defend with my very sharp horns" — The point, crossguard, and pommel all serve as offensive and defensive tools.

**Against Grips:** "I am against the other grips" — Bicorno is particularly effective when fighting in close quarters (narrow play) where grappling is likely.

**Versatile Weapon:** In this grip, you can use the point to thrust, the crossguard to hook, and the pommel to strike.

## **The Half-Sword Grip** {#the-half-sword-grip}

Bicorno introduces the concept of "half-swording" (*mezza spada*):

* One hand on the handle (normal position)  
* One hand grips the blade itself (typically with gauntlets or gloves)  
* Provides increased control and leverage  
* Allows for more precise thrusting  
* Effective against armored opponents  
* Essential for close-quarters combat

**Safety Note:** When practicing without gloves, grip the flat of the blade, not the edge. Apply gentle pressure only.

## **Connection to the Four Virtues** {#connection-to-the-four-virtues-7}

* **Elephant (Fortitude):** The stable, overhead position provides rock-solid defense  
* **Lion (Audacity):** Closing to half-sword range requires courage and commitment  
* **Tiger (Celerity):** The shortened lever allows for quick, precise movements  
* **Lynx (Prudence):** Multiple simultaneous threats require careful observation of which one the opponent fears most

## **Drill 1: Basic Structure and Half-Sword Grip** {#drill-1:-basic-structure-and-half-sword-grip}

**Solo Practice:**

1. Adopt forward-weighted stance  
2. Grip the sword handle with your rear (right) hand normally  
3. Grip the blade with your front (left) hand, approximately halfway up  
4. Raise the sword overhead, arms forming a "V"  
5. Point aims forward from above your head  
6. Hold for 10 seconds, checking: stable structure, comfortable grip, point control  
7. Repeat 10 times

**Safety Check:**

* Front hand grips the flat of the blade, not the edge  
* Grip is firm but not clenched  
* Shoulders are relaxed, not hunched

**Partner Practice:**

1. Partner A assumes Bicorno  
2. Partner B applies gentle downward pressure on A's blade  
3. A maintains structure without collapsing  
4. Note how the half-sword grip provides excellent leverage  
5. Switch roles

## **Drill 2: Thrusting from Bicorno** {#drill-2:-thrusting-from-bicorno}

With the half-sword grip, thrusts become more controlled and powerful.

**Solo:**

1. Adopt Bicorno (overhead, half-sword)  
2. Step forward and deliver a thrust downward and forward  
3. The front hand guides the blade with precision  
4. The rear hand provides power  
5. Return to Bicorno  
6. Repeat 10 times

**Partner (Slow, No Contact):**

1. Partner A in Bicorno  
2. Partner B in any guard  
3. A delivers slow, controlled thrust  
4. B observes the precision and control  
5. Switch roles

**Key Point:** The half-sword thrust is more controlled than a single-handed thrust. You can adjust the angle and direction mid-strike.

## **Drill 3: Defending Overhead** {#drill-3:-defending-overhead}

Bicorno provides excellent protection against overhead attacks.

**Partner (Slow):**

1. Partner A in Bicorno  
2. Partner B delivers slow overhead strikes (fendente)  
3. A uses the crossed position to catch and deflect the strike  
4. A's blade and arms form a strong defensive structure  
5. After deflecting, A can immediately thrust  
6. Repeat 10 times, then switch roles

**Teaching Point:** The overhead position creates a "roof" that protects against high attacks. The half-sword grip provides leverage to control the opponent's blade.

## **Common Errors** {#common-errors-14}

**Error:** Gripping the edge instead of the flat

**Fix:** Always grip the flat of the blade; rotate your front hand to avoid the edge

**Error:** Front hand too close to the handle

**Fix:** Move the front hand further up the blade (approximately halfway); this provides better leverage

**Error:** Hunched shoulders

**Fix:** Keep shoulders relaxed; the structure should be stable but not tense

**Error:** Point aiming too high or too low

**Fix:** Point should aim forward from above your head, roughly at opponent's face height

**Error:** Treating this as a purely defensive guard

**Fix:** Remember the "two horns" — this guard has multiple offensive options

# **Lesson 11: Posta Frontale / Corona (Frontal Guard / Crown)** {#lesson-11:-posta-frontale-/-corona-(frontal-guard-/-crown)}

**Classification:** Instabile (Mutable)

## **Getty Manuscript Text** {#getty-manuscript-text-10}

*"Posta frontale, over corona son, che offender e defender voglio. Lo mio parlare a tutti quanti scopro. Che chi me pora zenzer in punta tal conseglio, de ben ferir poy piuy non li scopro."*

**Translation:**

"I am Frontal Guard, or Crown, for I want to offend and defend. I reveal my speech to everyone. For whoever can cross me point to point, such counsel, to strike well after, I reveal to him no more."

## **Physical Description** {#physical-description-17}

\[**INSERT GETTY IMAGE: Folio 28r**\]

**Body Position:**

* Upright stance, weight neutral or slightly rear  
* Sword held high overhead, nearly vertical  
* Point aims upward and slightly forward  
* Arms extended upward, forming a "crown" shape  
* Very exposed-looking position

**Hand Position:**

* Both hands on the handle (standard grip)  
* Arms raised high, almost straight  
* Sword vertical or slightly angled forward  
* Hands approximately at forehead height or above

**The Name:** "Frontal Guard" (*Posta Frontale*) refers to facing the opponent directly with the body open. "Crown" (*Corona*) describes the high, overhead position that resembles a crown.

## **Tactical Function** {#tactical-function-17}

**Offend and Defend:** "I want to offend and defend" — Despite appearing purely defensive, this guard has offensive potential.

**Revealing:** "I reveal my speech to everyone" — This is an open, transparent guard. You're not hiding your intentions.

**Baiting:** "For whoever can cross me point to point...I reveal to him no more" — Like Coda Longa, this guard baits the opponent into attacking, then punishes them.

**Point Against Point:** The guard is designed to counter an opponent who tries to thrust into the high line.

## **The Deceptive Openness** {#the-deceptive-openness}

Corona appears to offer many openings:

* The body is completely exposed  
* The sword is out of the way, pointing up  
* You seem vulnerable to any attack

In reality:

* The high position allows fast descending strikes  
* The open body is bait — you want the opponent to attack  
* You can quickly transition to any other guard  
* The vertical blade can rotate to deflect from multiple angles

## **Connection to the Four Virtues** {#connection-to-the-four-virtues-8}

* **Lynx (Prudence):** Observing which attack your apparent vulnerability draws out  
* **Lion (Audacity):** Requires tremendous courage to adopt such an exposed position  
* **Tiger (Celerity):** The high position allows explosive descending strikes  
* **Elephant (Fortitude):** Despite appearing weak, the structure is balanced and stable

## **Drill 1: Basic Structure** {#drill-1:-basic-structure-3}

**Solo Practice:**

1. Adopt neutral stance  
2. Raise sword overhead, nearly vertical  
3. Point aims upward and slightly forward  
4. Arms extended, hands high  
5. Hold for 10 seconds  
6. Feel simultaneously exposed and ready  
7. Repeat 10 times

**Partner Practice:**

1. Partner A assumes Corona  
2. Partner B observes from different angles  
3. B describes what targets appear open  
4. A maintains the guard while B circles  
5. Switch roles

**Discussion:** After this drill, discuss how the open posture affects the opponent psychologically. What do they want to do when they see this guard?

## **Drill 2: Descending Strike from Corona** {#drill-2:-descending-strike-from-corona}

The natural strike from Corona is a powerful descending cut.

**Solo:**

1. Corona (sword high, vertical)  
2. Step forward and bring the sword down in a powerful arc  
3. The high starting position generates tremendous momentum  
4. Finish in Dente di Zenghiaro or Porta di Ferro Mezana (lower guards)  
5. Repeat 10 times

**Partner (Slow):**

1. Partner A in Corona  
2. Partner B in any guard  
3. A delivers slow descending strike  
4. B observes the long arc and speed potential  
5. Switch roles

**Key Point:** The strike from Corona travels farther and builds more momentum than from most other guards.

## **Drill 3: Baiting and Counter-Striking** {#drill-3:-baiting-and-counter-striking}

**Partner (Light Contact):**

1. A adopts Corona (appearing vulnerable)  
2. B in any aggressive guard  
3. B sees the openings and advances to strike  
4. As B commits, A brings the sword down to intercept and strike simultaneously  
5. A's goal: land your strike while defending against theirs  
6. Repeat 10 times, then switch roles

**Teaching Point:** Corona is a "trap" guard similar to Coda Longa. You bait the attack, then punish it.

## **Common Errors** {#common-errors-15}

**Error:** Leaning backward or retreating

**Fix:** Stay balanced and upright; don't actually retreat despite the guard's defensive appearance

**Error:** Blade angled too far forward

**Fix:** Keep the blade more vertical; it should point upward, not at the opponent

**Error:** Dropping the arms from fatigue

**Fix:** Practice holding the position for longer periods; build endurance

**Error:** Passive mindset

**Fix:** Remember this guard baits attacks; you're actively setting a trap, not passively defending

# **Lesson 12: Dente di Zenghiaro Mezana (Middle Boar's Tooth)** {#lesson-12:-dente-di-zenghiaro-mezana-(middle-boar's-tooth)}

**Classification:** Stabile (Stable)

## **Getty Manuscript Text** {#getty-manuscript-text-11}

The Getty manuscript does not provide separate text for Dente di Zenghiaro Mezana, as it is a variation of Dente di Zenghiaro / Tutta Porta di Ferro from Lesson 3\. The tactical principles are similar, but applied from a middle height position.

## **Physical Description** {#physical-description-18}

\[**INSERT GETTY IMAGE: Middle Zenghiaro variation**\]

**Body Position:**

* Forward-weighted or neutral stance  
* Sword held at middle height (higher than Tutta Porta di Ferro, lower than Porta di Ferro Mezana)  
* Point aims forward and upward  
* Hands approximately at waist or lower chest height  
* Stable, grounded posture

**Hand Position:**

* Hands at mid-height, between hip and chest  
* Comfortable, natural grip  
* Point threatens the centerline

**The Name:** "Middle Boar's Tooth" positions this guard between Tutta Porta di Ferro (low, hands at hip) and Porta di Ferro Mezana (middle, hands at chest).

## **Tactical Function** {#tactical-function-18}

All tactical functions from Dente di Zenghiaro / Tutta Porta di Ferro apply, but from a middle height:

**Defensive Strength:** Strong structural defense from the middle position

**Thrust Beating:** Can deflect thrusts downward, similar to the low version

**Closing Distance:** Effective for entering close range

**Covering:** Every action includes a defensive element

**Middle Height Advantage:** Can defend against both high and low attacks more easily than the extreme low position

## **Why Three "Iron Doors"?** {#why-three-"iron-doors"?}

Fiore provides three variations of Porta di Ferro at different heights:

1. **Tutta Porta di Ferro (Full Iron Door)** — Low position, hands at hip  
2. **Porta di Ferro Mezana (Middle Iron Door)** — Middle position, hands at chest  
3. **Dente di Zenghiaro Mezana (Middle Boar's Tooth)** — Between low and middle

These three positions form a vertical continuum of stable, defensive guards. Together, they allow you to defend any height while maintaining the "iron door" stability.

## **Connection to the Four Virtues** {#connection-to-the-four-virtues-9}

* **Elephant (Fortitude):** Like all Porta di Ferro variations, this embodies defensive stability  
* **Tiger (Celerity):** The middle position allows quick transition to high or low guards  
* **Lynx (Prudence):** Balanced position supports careful observation  
* **Lion (Audacity):** Despite being defensive, must be ready to strike decisively

## **Drill 1: Three Iron Doors Flow** {#drill-1:-three-iron-doors-flow}

This drill integrates all three Porta di Ferro variations.

**Solo:**

1. Start in Dente di Zenghiaro / Tutta Porta di Ferro (low, hands at hip)  
2. Raise to Dente di Zenghiaro Mezana (middle-low, hands at waist)  
3. Raise to Porta di Ferro Mezana (middle, hands at chest)  
4. Descend back through Mezana (middle-low)  
5. Return to Tutta Porta di Ferro (low)  
6. Repeat the circuit 10 times smoothly

**Key Point:** These are not separate, unrelated guards — they're points on a continuum. You can slide between them fluidly.

**Partner:**

1. A flows between the three Iron Door positions  
2. B delivers slow attacks at various heights (high, middle, low)  
3. A adjusts to the appropriate Iron Door height to defend  
4. Repeat 10 times, then switch roles

## **Drill 2: Closing from the Middle** {#drill-2:-closing-from-the-middle}

**Partner (Light Contact):**

1. A in Dente di Zenghiaro Mezana  
2. B in extended guard (Longa, Fenestra)  
3. A uses the middle Iron Door position to deflect B's point  
4. A steps forward, closing distance while maintaining cover  
5. A finishes in close quarters (narrow play)  
6. Repeat 10 times, then switch roles

**Key Point:** The middle height is ideal for closing distance — high enough to control extended points, low enough to stay under high guards.

## **Common Errors** {#common-errors-16}

**Error:** Confusing the three Iron Door heights

**Fix:** Practice the Three Iron Doors Flow drill until the distinctions are clear

**Error:** Point dropping

**Fix:** At all three heights, the point should aim forward and upward, threatening the opponent

**Error:** Treating the middle variations as less important

**Fix:** All three Iron Doors are essential; the middle positions are not "compromises" but specific tactical choices
