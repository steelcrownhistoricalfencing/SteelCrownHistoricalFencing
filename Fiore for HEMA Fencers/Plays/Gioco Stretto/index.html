    Gioco Stretto — The Close Game

    Zogho Stretto

    The Close Game begins when the distance collapses.

    When blades meet at mid-sword or near the hilt, when bodies are close enough to touch, you are in stretto. The rules of wide play no longer apply. Cuts and thrusts at full extension are not available. What is available is everything else.

    At close measure, the sword becomes a lever, a hook, and a hammer. The blade is the last of its uses.

    What Defines the Close Game

    Three things identify stretto:

    The blades cross at the mezza spada or tutta, mid-blade or near the hilt.

    The right foot is forward, and the body is already committed into close range.

    You are close enough to reach your opponent's body with your hands, your pommel, or your hilt.

    At this distance, the blade is too long to swing freely. Attempting a full cut risks hitting nothing. The sword becomes a different tool.

    How You Enter Stretto

    No one enters the close game by choice in isolation. You arrive there.

    The most common entry: a largo play resolves with the right foot passing forward. The exchange has closed the distance, and the foot position tells you which game follows.

    A deliberate passing step forward can also bring you to stretto range. Some guards, particularly Tutta Porta di Ferro, generate a crossing at the hilt directly, placing both fencers immediately in the close game.

    However you arrive, the decision to act in stretto must be immediate. The position is unstable. A fencer who pauses in stretto without acting gives the opponent time to recover.

    The Three Entry Techniques

    Entering stretto does not mean anything is available. It means one of three entries is available, depending on which side the bind is on.

    The Pommel Strike — Colpo di Pomo — enters from the mandritto (forehand) side. The sword rolls under the bind and the pommel drives toward the opponent's face.

    The Arm Grab — seizing the opponent's weapon arm at the wrist or forearm. This can lead to joint locks (ligadure), disarms, and throws.

    The Sword Wrap — guiding the blade around and behind the opponent's neck. From this position, a pull cuts the throat and sets up a throw.

    These three entries are not interchangeable. Which one applies depends on the side of the bind and the specific position at the moment of entry.

    The Mandritto and Riverso Sides

    Every stretto situation has a dominant side determined by the bind.

    Mandritto, the forehand side, is when the contact is on your right, your strong side. From a mandritto bind, the pommel strike is the primary entry.

    Riverso, the backhand side, is when the contact is on your left, your weak side. From a riverso bind, a wrap is typically required before any technique can be executed cleanly.

    Identifying which side you are on happens automatically with practice, but it must be trained explicitly at first. The wrong entry from the wrong side creates neither technique nor control.

    What Is Available in Stretto

    The full range of stretto techniques includes:

    • Pommel strikes (colpo di pomo)
    • Joint locks (ligadura sotana, mezana, soprana)
    • Disarms (disarmare)
    • Throws and takedowns (gettare, abbattere)
    • Throat cuts from blade wraps
    • Elbow strikes and pushing actions

    These do not happen in isolation. They chain. The pommel strike creates the position for a joint lock. The joint lock creates the position for a throw. The throw may end in a disarm.

    A stretto sequence does not end until someone is on the ground, disarmed, or the distance reopens.

    The Abrazare Connection

    Fiore's stretto plays do not exist in isolation. They connect directly to his wrestling section, abrazare, which appears earlier in the manuscript and covers grappling principles in their own right.

    The joint locks, throws, and close-range body control used in stretto are drawn directly from abrazare. The two systems share vocabulary and mechanics.

    A fencer who has studied only the longsword plays and not the wrestling will find the stretto plays harder to understand. The arm control, the leverage principles, and the sensitivity to balance disruption all come from abrazare.

    The Close Game Is Not Safe to Leave

    Fiore is explicit: once in stretto, you are not in a safe position to pause.

    If you find yourself in close range without a technique, the opponent is also in close range with a technique available to them.

    The choice is: act in stretto, or disengage and return to largo.

    Disengaging from stretto requires a step back and a re-extension of the blade to reestablish largo range. This is not a failure, sometimes it is the correct decision. But it must be active and deliberate, not passive.

    Plays in This Section

    Pommel Strike — Colpo di Pomo — The primary entry into stretto from the mandritto side. The sword pivots around the bind and the pommel strikes the face.

    Lower Bind — Ligadura Sotana — A joint lock applied with the arm forced downward. The first of the three locks.

    Middle Bind — Ligadura Mezana — A joint lock at shoulder height, applied through a spiraling arm wrap. The mechanical foundation of all three locks.

    Upper Bind — Ligadura Soprana — A joint lock that drives the arm high, leading naturally into a throw.

    Disarms — Disarmare — The sequence of releasing your own sword to take the opponent's.

    Throws and Takedowns — Gettare / Abbattere — Takedowns that emerge from established stretto plays.

    Key Idea

    The close game is where the longsword becomes something other than a blade.

    It becomes a control surface, a striking tool, and a lever.

    Enter with intention, act immediately, and chain to a resolution. There is no neutral in stretto.
