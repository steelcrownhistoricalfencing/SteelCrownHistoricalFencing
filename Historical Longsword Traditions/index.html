    Historical Longsword Traditions

    European longsword was never a single, unified martial art. Over several centuries, different masters developed distinct systems of fencing, each shaped by its own region, culture, and purpose. While they all used the longsword, they often solved the same problems in very different ways.

    Today, four traditions are particularly influential within Historical European Martial Arts (HEMA).

    Italian Armizare

    The Martial Art of Fiore dei Liberi
    Region Northern Italy
    Period c. 1400–1410
    Primary Source Fiore dei Liberi's Fior di Battaglia ("Flower of Battle")

    Armizare is one of the oldest complete martial systems preserved in Europe. Rather than teaching only swordsmanship, Fiore presents a complete curriculum for the professional man-at-arms.

    Students begin with wrestling before progressing through dagger, one-handed sword, longsword, spear, pollaxe, mounted combat, and armored fighting. Each weapon builds upon principles learned in the previous one, creating a cohesive martial art rather than separate disciplines.

    Philosophy

    Armizare emphasizes controlling the opponent rather than overpowering them.

    Key principles include timing, measure, balance, leverage, body mechanics, and the crossing of swords (incrosada). Rather than relying on speed alone, Fiore teaches students to safely gain control before delivering decisive attacks.

    Characteristics

    • Strong integration of wrestling
    • Heavy emphasis on thrusting
    • Universal principles across all weapons
    • Compact, efficient guard system
    • Equal focus on offense and defense
    • Extensive armored techniques

    Best Known For

    Grappling with the sword, counterattacks, blade control, tactical versatility.

    How It Shows Up in Modern HEMA

    Armizare is one of the most widely studied traditions in modern HEMA. The Getty manuscript is fully available through the J. Paul Getty Museum's open content program, and the Wiktenauer project provides transcriptions and translations of all known Fiore manuscripts.

    Fiore's complete system is particularly attractive to modern practitioners because it addresses so many situations within a single coherent framework. The wrestling foundation means stretto techniques carry real mechanical weight, and the integration across weapons means a longsword student can find direct connections to dagger and pollaxe without learning an entirely new system.

    In competition, Fiore-trained fencers are well represented at major international tournaments. The stretto game in particular gives them options that purely largo-focused competitors often lack.

    At Steel Crown, Armizare is the foundation of our longsword curriculum.

    The German Kunst des Fechtens

    The Tradition of Johannes Liechtenauer
    Region Holy Roman Empire
    Period Late 1300s–1500s
    Primary Masters Johannes Liechtenauer, Sigmund Ringeck, Peter von Danzig, Hans Talhoffer

    Rather than a single fencing manual, the German tradition consists of dozens of fencing books based upon the teachings of Johannes Liechtenauer. These masters expanded and explained his original verses over the following two centuries.

    Philosophy

    The German school revolves around initiative. Instead of waiting for an opponent, practitioners seek to seize control through aggressive attacks and intelligent use of the bind.

    Central concepts include Vor (Before), Nach (After), Indes (In the instant), and Fühlen (Feeling through the bind). Much of the system focuses on reading pressure after the swords make contact and immediately responding.

    Characteristics

    • Master Cuts (Meisterhäue)
    • Extensive winding techniques
    • Dynamic blade engagement
    • Powerful descending cuts
    • Aggressive tactical mindset

    Best Known For

    Blade work, bind fighting, offensive pressure, tactical initiative.

    How It Shows Up in Modern HEMA

    The German tradition is the most widely practiced longsword system in HEMA today. The sheer volume of source material spanning over a century means there is enough material to study for a lifetime, and an active international community of researchers continues to produce new translations and interpretations.

    Most major HEMA clubs teach some form of KdF, and many international tournament rulesets were developed with the German tradition in mind. The emphasis on aggressive bind work and initiative aligns naturally with competitive scoring structures that reward decisive action.

    The volume of source material is also a challenge. Different masters within the tradition disagree on details, and interpretations vary significantly between schools. This makes KdF a rich area of scholarly debate as well as practical training.

    The Italian Tradition of Filippo Vadi

    The Bridge Between Medieval and Renaissance Fencing
    Region Italy
    Period 1482–1487
    Primary Source Filippo Vadi's De Arte Gladiatoria Dimicandi

    Vadi wrote roughly seventy years after Fiore and is often considered the natural evolution of the Armizare tradition. His work shares many of Fiore's concepts while introducing new tactical ideas and refinements.

    Philosophy

    Where Fiore emphasizes universal martial principles, Vadi often focuses more on precision and fencing at longer measure. His writing encourages patience, precision, point control, geometric movement, and tactical positioning.

    Characteristics

    • Longer fighting distance
    • Increased emphasis on thrusts
    • More geometric movement
    • Refined guard transitions
    • Elegant tactical flow

    Best Known For

    Precision fencing, long measure, technical point work.

    Many modern practitioners view Vadi as a transitional system between medieval knightly combat and later Renaissance fencing.

    How It Shows Up in Modern HEMA

    Vadi is most commonly studied alongside Fiore as a comparative system rather than as a standalone tradition. Because his work shares so much vocabulary with Armizare, practitioners who already know Fiore find Vadi illuminating: his refinements and divergences help clarify which elements of Fiore are core principles and which are interpretive choices.

    Dedicated Vadi practitioners exist, but the tradition does not have the same tournament presence or club infrastructure as Fiore or the German school. It is primarily a system for students who want to go deeper into the Italian tradition.

    Joachim Meyer

    The Renaissance German School
    Region Germany
    Period 1570
    Primary Source Joachim Meyer's Gründtliche Beschreibung der Kunst des Fechtens

    Although Meyer belongs to the Liechtenauer tradition, his fencing reflects a changing world. By the late 16th century, judicial duels and battlefield combat had become less common, and public fencing schools were increasingly popular. Meyer's work preserves the German tradition while adapting it for civilian training and competition.

    Philosophy

    Meyer teaches students to become complete fencers through structured drills, flowing combinations, and continuous movement. His system values control, rhythm, fluidity, technical development, and athleticism.

    Characteristics

    • Long attack combinations
    • Continuous movement
    • Extensive cutting drills
    • Flow between guards
    • Highly structured instruction

    Best Known For

    Beautiful cutting mechanics, training methodology, fluid combinations, athletic fencing.

    How It Shows Up in Modern HEMA

    Meyer has become one of the most practically influential systems in modern HEMA competition. His emphasis on cutting mechanics, footwork patterns, and extended combinations translates directly into tournament scoring, and many competitors who primarily train other systems incorporate Meyer's drills as a technical development tool.

    His structured training methodology also makes him unusually accessible for club environments. The drills are clearly described, progression is logical, and the emphasis on flow means students develop athleticism and body awareness alongside technical skill.

    It is worth noting that Meyer's work is sometimes criticized as representing a sport fencing context rather than a martial one. His system was explicitly developed for civilian training and competition, which means some of the lethality and practicality that characterize earlier systems is less present. This is not a flaw for modern practitioners, but it is useful context.

    Iberian Longsword

    The Portuguese and Spanish Traditions
    Region Portugal and Spain
    Period Late 1500s–1600s
    Primary Masters Domingo Luis Godinho, Diogo Gomes de Figueyredo

    The Iberian traditions represent some of the latest surviving systems to teach the two-handed sword. Their works were written during the Renaissance, when firearms and the rapier were becoming increasingly common, yet they preserved sophisticated methods for using the longsword and the montante (greatsword).

    Philosophy

    Iberian fencing emphasizes intelligent movement, controlling space, and adapting to changing situations. Rather than relying on direct blade engagement, these systems often focus on creating tactical advantages through positioning and timing.

    Characteristics

    • Dynamic footwork
    • Tactical use of distance
    • Relationship to montante fencing
    • Practical civilian applications
    • Renaissance-era tactics

    Best Known For

    Movement, spatial awareness, versatility, greatsword traditions.

    How It Shows Up in Modern HEMA

    The Iberian traditions are a smaller but growing presence in modern HEMA. Portuguese and Spanish practitioners have driven much of the recent research into Godinho and Figueyredo, and the community is active in producing translations and interpretations of the primary sources.

    The montante in particular has attracted international attention. Godinho's system of fourteen rules for the greatsword is among the most complete surviving treatments of the weapon, and groups across Europe and the Americas now train it regularly.

    At Steel Crown, we train Godinho's Montante alongside the Fiore longsword curriculum. The two systems complement each other: Fiore establishes the foundational principles, and Godinho extends them into a different weapon and tactical context.

    A Shared Heritage

    While each of these traditions has its own personality, they share a common foundation. All teach that success comes not from strength alone, but from disciplined practice, intelligent movement, and sound martial principles.

    At Steel Crown, our curriculum is rooted in Fiore dei Liberi's Armizare, but we value and respect the broader European martial traditions that enrich our understanding of historical fencing.
