# Opposition and Cover

Not all defenses are equal.

A parry that deflects an attack has stopped one threat. A parry that controls the line has stopped all threats from that direction while creating an immediate counter-opportunity. These are different actions with different outcomes, and the distinction between them matters every time blades meet.

**Opposition closes a line with your blade while your point threatens. Cover only closes the line.**

---

## The Distinction

**Cover** is a defensive action that moves an incoming weapon off its target. The attack is deflected. The line is cleared for that moment. But nothing else is established. The opponent can recover their blade, change lines, and attack again. Cover buys time.

**Opposition** is a defensive action that controls the incoming weapon by maintaining contact and pressure while your own point angles toward the opponent's target. The attack is not just deflected — it is held. The opponent cannot recover their blade freely because yours is against it and your weight is behind it. Opposition closes one threat and creates another simultaneously.

The Exchange of Thrusts is the clearest example in the curriculum. The offline step takes your body away from the incoming thrust. The crossing of the blades is not a deflection — your arms extend low and outward to collect the incoming blade while your point rises toward the opponent's face. Both happen in one motion. The attack is opposed, not just parried. You are threatening before their attack has finished.

---

## What Makes Opposition Work

Opposition requires three things to be present at the same time:

**Contact with the incoming weapon.** Your blade must be against theirs with enough pressure to control its path. Light contact deflects. Maintained contact controls.

**Weight behind the blade.** Opposition is not an arm movement. The force that holds the opponent's blade in check comes from body alignment and weight transfer, not from grip strength. A fencer who opposes with the arms alone will be pushed off by a stronger opponent. A fencer who opposes with their structure behind the blade will hold the line regardless of size difference.

**Point threatening a target.** If your blade controls theirs but your point is aimed at the ceiling, you have cover, not opposition. The point must be on a line that forces the opponent to respond to it. This is what makes opposition a simultaneous action — defense and offense exist at the same moment.

---

## Cover Has Its Place

Cover is not wrong. There are situations where deflection is the correct choice.

Breaking the Thrust uses a beat, which is closer to a forceful cover than a true opposition. The blade is knocked aside and the body moves to exploit the moment before it recovers. This is two-time: cover, then counter. It works because the body movement is fast enough that the gap between the two actions is negligible.

The distinction is that opposition collapses the two actions into one. When one-time action is available, it is preferable. When the geometry or timing makes it unavailable, two-time cover and counter is the correct response.

Training to prefer opposition when possible builds the habit of simultaneous action. Training to accept cover when necessary builds tactical flexibility.

---

## Opposition in Stretto

Opposition continues to matter at close range, though it changes form.

In stretto, the weapons are no longer crossing near the tips. The contact is mid-blade or at the hilt. The mechanical principle is the same: pressure against the opponent's weapon arm or blade while your own action continues toward its target.

The pommel strike is an opposition in this sense. The bind is the contact. The roll under the bind and the strike of the pommel happen together. The blade controls the line while the pommel acts.

---

## In Practice

When reviewing a defensive action that failed, ask whether it was cover or opposition.

If the opponent hit you after your parry, the parry may have been cover that deflected without controlling. Their blade recovered before yours returned to threaten. The fix is not a harder parry. It is an earlier point — threatening before they can recover.

If your opposition failed, the cause is almost always weight. The arms held the line but the body was not behind them. The fix is to feel where your mass is when you make contact with the blade, and make sure it is moving toward the opponent, not away.
