# Inside and Outside Line

Where your weapon is relative to your opponent's weapon determines what both of you can do.

This is not a subtle point. It is probably the single most practically useful spatial concept in fencing. Students who understand it make better decisions in sparring immediately. Students who do not understand it repeat the same errors indefinitely without knowing why.

**Your position relative to your opponent's weapon is the first thing to read in any exchange.**

---

## The Definition

The **inside line** is the space between your opponent's weapon and their centerline. If your blade is on the inside line, it is positioned between their guard and the center of their body.

The **outside line** is the space on the far side of their weapon arm. If your blade is on the outside line, it is positioned away from their centerline, on the side of their weapon hand or beyond it.

A simpler way to feel the distinction: on the inside line, your blade and their blade are in the same general space. On the outside line, your blade has cleared their weapon entirely and is approaching from a direction they cannot easily address with it.

---

## Why It Matters

From the **inside line**, both weapons are competing for the same space. Your attack and their parry are likely to meet. Your thrust goes toward the center of their body, but their blade can reach it. You can threaten directly, but so can they.

From the **outside line**, their weapon cannot easily reach your blade without a significant change of position. You are attacking from an angle their guard does not naturally cover. This is why gaining the outside line is a central goal in most fencing systems — not because it is safe, but because it limits what the opponent can do in response.

The Exchange of Thrusts works precisely because the offline step moves your body to the outside line while your blade returns on the inside. Your target is exposed. Theirs is not.

---

## How Lines Change

Lines are not fixed. They shift constantly as the exchange develops.

A step offline can move you from the inside to the outside line in a single motion. A crossing of the blades can reverse which fencer holds which line. A beat or deflection that moves the opponent's blade past center puts them on the outside line.

The goal is not to be on the outside line once. It is to understand which line you currently hold and make decisions accordingly.

---

## Common Errors

**Attacking from the inside line without controlling the opponent's weapon.** This is the most common beginner error. The attack is direct and the target is real, but the opponent's weapon can reach you just as directly. Both fencers hit each other. The solution is either to move to the outside line first, or to control the opponent's weapon before committing.

**Gaining the outside line and then not committing.** A student steps offline, has a clear outside line, and then hesitates or reverts to a technique that works from the inside. The advantage evaporates because it was not used.

**Confusing outside line with wide measure.** The outside line is about the angle of your weapon relative to theirs, not about how far away you are. You can be on the outside line at stretto distance. A disarm from Fiore's stretto section often requires being on the outside line of the weapon arm at extremely close range.

---

## Connection to the Fiore Curriculum

Every largo play in the Fiore curriculum involves a line decision.

Breaking the Thrust gains the outside line by stepping left while beating the blade to that side. The Peasant's Blow exits the inside line when the opponent's strike carries past center, leaving the Scholar on the effective outside of a now-committed attack.

In stretto, the joint locks require an outside position on the weapon arm specifically. The Lower Bind, Middle Bind, and Upper Bind all fail if applied from the inside. The outside position is not a preference, it is a mechanical requirement.

Reading the plays with this concept in mind makes the spatial logic of each technique clear.
